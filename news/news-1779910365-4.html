Japan officials visit sanctioned Russia to protect corporate assets

Senior officials from the trade and foreign ministries are expected to meet with Russian government representatives during a business trip to the country, which is under Japanese economic sanctions over its invasion of Ukraine, the trade minister said.

Ryosei Akazawa told a news conference on May 26 that Japan is communicating with Russia to prevent the assets of Japanese companies operating there since prior to the invasion from being subjected to unjust treatment such as seizures.

“We make a point of sending officials several times a year to make representations,” he said.

Japan deems Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 a violation of international law. It has imposed import-export bans and financial sanctions on Moscow in coordination with other Group of Seven members.

Visiting Russia are Masayoshi Arai, director-general of the Trade Policy Bureau at the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry, and Masaki Ishikawa, deputy director-general of the European Affairs Bureau at the Foreign Ministry.

The Trade Policy Bureau oversees trade negotiations and economic partnerships with foreign countries, while the European Affairs Bureau handles relations with European nations, including Russia.

While the detailed itinerary is unclear, the two are scheduled to meet with officials in charge of trade within the Russian government, sources said.

Representatives from Japanese companies operating in Russia may attend.

Keidanren (Japan Business Federation) has also dispatched a senior official.

One factor behind the trip appears to be the government’s belief that maintaining communication with Russia is necessary from the perspective of diversifying Japan’s energy procurement.

Japan, which heavily relies on the Middle East for oil and gas supplies, has been hit hard by the U.S.-Israeli conflict with Iran and the de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Economic sanctions do not apply to liquefied natural gas produced at Sakhalin-2, a Russian energy development project in which major Japanese trading houses hold stakes, and crude oil generated during the LNG production process.

With tensions in the Middle East escalating, the relative importance of these resources has increased.

In early May, Japan imported Russian crude oil from the project for the first time since the Strait of Hormuz was effectively closed.

Additionally, there are indications that Japan is looking ahead to rebuilding relations with Russia once the invasion ends.

Akazawa denied that the trip has any “intention to pursue new economic cooperation.”

Still, Japan once had deep relations with Russia, with many Japanese companies operating factories in the country.

“We cannot sever ties with Russia,” a senior METI official said.

On the Russian side, too, there are signs of an intent to advance consultations with Japan.

Russia’s Foreign Ministry issued a statement on May 14, soon after the latest trip was first reported, emphasizing its stance that it is necessary to lift sanctions against Moscow.

“If the Japanese government wishes to protect the interests of companies, it should start by restoring a normal political environment,” it said.

While noting that Russia would not be the one to call for talks, the statement said it is prepared to engage if Japan requests contact.

One presumed aim is to drive a wedge into the G-7 over anti-Russian sanctions.

While the European Union aims to phase out Russian fossil fuels, Japan intends to continue importing LNG and other resources, making it easier for Russia to foster an environment conducive to dialogue.

“Japan has imposed sanctions, but Japanese companies may continue their activities here,” Russian President Vladimir Putin said in 2024. “We are prepared to build relations with Japan for the next five or even 50 years.”

Under Russia’s wartime economy, limits to growth have emerged. Ukrainian attacks have also reduced its capacity to export oil and other resources.

With growing economic reliance on China, Russia appears to be seeking to revitalize its economy by rebuilding ties with Japan.
