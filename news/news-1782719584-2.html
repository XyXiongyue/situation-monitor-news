SunSirs: Where Are International Gold and Crude Oil Prices Headed?

Sun, 28 Jun 2026 20:25

According to the Economic Information Daily, prices for crude oil and gold in international markets recently fell in tandem, briefly dropping below key round-number thresholds. However, following a flare-up in military hostilities between the U.S. and Iran on the 26th, prices for both commodities rebounded; their future trajectory warrants close attention.

Mid-last week, the price of August futures for New York light sweet crude oil briefly dipped below the $70 mark, while August futures for London Brent crude fell to their lowest level since early March. Compared to the recent peak of $119.48, international oil prices have dropped by more than 40%—with a decline of over 20% in the past month alone—effectively erasing the risk premium associated with Middle East geopolitical tensions and returning prices to pre-conflict levels.

International gold prices have also steadily declined, falling more than 25% since early March.

The recent simultaneous drop in gold and crude oil prices stems from both shared drivers and factors specific to each commodity.

Regarding shared drivers: First, the easing of Middle East geopolitical tensions—the very factor that had triggered the earlier surge in both gold and oil prices—caused a collapse in the market's "safe-haven premium."

Oil prices had previously spiked—with New York light sweet crude hitting a high of $119.48 per barrel—amid market panic over a potential blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and a disruption in crude supplies. Since June, however, as the U.S. and Iran entered a second round of negotiations, shipping through the strait fully resumed, and the supply crisis was averted, oil prices surrendered those gains and fell back to their pre-conflict range.

In the international gold market, prices had already begun to retreat during the Middle East conflict but remained volatile due to safe-haven buying. Once geopolitical risks subsided, safe-haven capital exited rapidly and positions were liquidated; consequently, prices briefly fell below the $4,000 mark during trading last week.

Second, market expectations shifted from Federal Reserve rate cuts to rate hikes; this drove a surge in real yields on U.S. Treasury bonds and a rise in the U.S. Dollar Index, exerting broad downward pressure on commodity prices.

At the Federal Reserve's June policy meeting, nearly half of the officials supported resuming interest rate hikes within the year, and the probability of a rate hike in September rose to 70%. Market expectations for a Federal Reserve rate cut within the year have completely vanished.

Although the easing of Middle East tensions alleviated upward pressure on U.S. Treasury yields, heightened expectations of Fed rate hikes kept yields elevated; during last week's trading, yields on 2-year, 3-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year U.S. Treasuries stood at 4.148%, 4.147%, 4.176%, 4.394%, and 4.844%, respectively.

Meanwhile, the U.S. Dollar Index recently hit a nearly 13-month high. Hovering around the 101.5 level last week—having gained over 3% since early May—the dollar's strength triggered a broad decline in prices for dollar-denominated commodities such as gold and crude oil; gold, silver, crude oil, copper, and aluminum all weakened across the board.

Of course, both commodities also face specific bearish factors.

For gold, the temporary easing of geopolitical tensions has caused inflation expectations to retreat, diminishing gold's value as an inflation hedge; additionally, after rising continuously for over two years, significant profit-taking by long-position holders at high price levels has led to a persistent price correction.

As for crude oil, on the supply side, Gulf nations are gradually lifting voluntary production cuts, leading to expectations of looser global supply; this, combined with the fact that Middle East tensions have driven U.S. shale oil production to new highs, has ensured a degree of supply stability. On the demand side, earlier conflicts negatively impacted consumption, while the persistence of high interest rates has stifled economic growth, limiting the strength of any demand recovery. Weak supply-demand fundamentals have resulted in rapid volatility in oil prices.

In the short term, market observers believe that a renewed outbreak of military hostilities between the U.S. and Iran introduces significant uncertainty into international gold and crude oil markets, potentially triggering sharp, two-way volatility. Should the conflict be prolonged—or effectively render the U.S.-Iran agreement a dead letter—gold and oil markets might undergo a repricing, and the global economy would lose a hard-won opportunity for recovery, making severe negative consequences likely unavoidable. If the US and Iran reach an agreement in the short term, the US dollar exchange rate and interest rate expectations for major economies will become the primary drivers of future gold and crude oil market trends; prices for both commodities may continue to fluctuate within a weak range, with the only potential catalysts for an upward move in oil prices being a renewed deterioration of the situation in the Middle East or fresh disruptions to shipping in the region's straits. In the long term, gold remains supported by continued buying from central banks, limiting its downside potential; meanwhile, the outlook for crude oil depends on factors such as policy shifts by "OPEC+" and the extent of actual demand generated by the global economic recovery, with the pace of demand recovery serving as the critical variable influencing the market's next phase.
