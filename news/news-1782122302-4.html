All eyes on Kevin Warsh: New Fed Chair keeps rate unchanged, hosts first news briefing

Wed, 17 Jun 2026 08:06

by JESSICA A. BOTELHO | The National News Desk

WASHINGTON (TNND) —New Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warshheld his first post-meeting news conference on Wednesday, with the Fed keeping its key rate unchanged at about 3.6%.

Warsh, who recently assumed leadership of the nation's central bank after Jerome Powell's departure from the position, discussed the Federal Open Market Committee's (FOMC) latest interest rate decision and answered questions from reporters about the economic outlook.

WASHINGTON, DC - JUNE 17: Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh speaks to reporters during his first news conference since taking the helm at the central bank on June 17, 2026 in Washington, DC. Warsh was appointed by President Donald Trump after former chair Jerome Powell's tenure ended in May. (Photo by Chip Somodevilla/Getty Images)

But almost half the central bank’s policymakers said they could support a rate hike later this year, an unexpectedly aggressive outcome that would disappoint President Donald Trump and suggests heightened concerns about persistent inflation.

Fed policymakers could change their post-meeting statement so that it no longer signals the central bank's next move will be to reduce interest rates. Such a change would suggest it could keep rates unchanged for an extended period -- or even raise them if inflation stays elevated.

Since the Iran war started on February 28, inflation has accelerated to a three-year high of 4.2%, lifted mostly by costlier gas. The Fed typically fights higher inflation by raising its key interest rate to cool spending and growth.

Trump on Sunday announced a peace agreementthat could bring the three-month conflict to an end, but it's not clear if peace will hold. And even if oil flows freely out of the Middle East again,it could take months for prices of gas, groceries, and items such as airline fares, to cool. Already, inflation according to the Fed's preferred measure has topped its 2% target for more than five years.

The appearance marked a significant milestone in Warsh's tenure, giving investors, economists, and policymakers their first extended opportunity to hear directly from the Fed chairman in a formal post-meeting setting.

The Federal Reserve has spent the past several years navigating persistent inflation pressures while seeking to maintain economic growth and employment. Any signals about the timing of future policy moves could influence markets ranging from stocks and bonds to mortgages and consumer loans.
