Countdown: CEO reflections on IMO progress and regulation’s role in maritime decarbonization

2026-05-26 18:34

inInternational Shipping News26/05/2026

When we started the Center in 2020, the industry was asking whether decarbonizing shipping was possible. Today, the ecosystem is no longer asking that question. Companies have set ambitious targets and are implementing low-emissions technologies in their fleets. We have moved from asking whether shipping can decarbonize to asking how and when.

In that time, we have also seen significant geopolitical shifts and changing market conditions that have posed real challenges for the transition. Against that backdrop, we have decided to make a strategic adjustment to our work at the Center to strengthen our impact on maritime decarbonization. This has required difficult decisions, including a reduction in our staff. We are grateful to every colleague who is no longer at the Center. Their work has contributed to the foundations and insight our organization needs to move forward with confidence.

Far from retreating, the Center is adapting so that we can continue to deliver on our mission, together with our partners and the wider maritime ecosystem. Drawing on the insights and experience we have gained, we are deepening our focus and directing our energy to where our impact can be greatest. We will concentrate our resources in two core areas where we see the greatest opportunity to deliver immediate and measurable results:

1.Fleet transition, focused on accelerating the maturation and adoption of energy efficiency measures and technology solutions for low-emissions power and propulsion (including fuel production, ship design and operation, bunkering/logistics, energy conversion, emissions monitoring and control)

2.Regulation and policy, focused on providing independent, science-based insights to inform industry standards, business frameworks, and global and regional regulatory development

As part of this work, we will continue to help set standards for new fuels and provide insights into their supply and demand. Furthermore, our work with green shipping corridors will help to shape policy interventions and financing mechanisms that can deliver real-world change.

That brings me to the current moment and the recent meetings at the IMO. When we started the latest Countdown series following the approval of the IMO Net-Zero Framework in April 2025, IMO Member States had just concluded months of negotiations, following years of work, on a package of measures that delicately balanced the interests of Member States. The Center supported this work through our insights and analysis shared through Countdown as well as directly with Member States. The resulting Net-Zero Framework included ambitious targets that provided needed clarity for the industry, incentives to support the uptake of low-emission fuels and technologies, and mechanisms to support a just and equitable transition. Some felt it went too far, others not far enough, but there was broad agreement among a large majority of Member States that the result represented a viable way forward.

Then came October 2025, when the adoption of the Net-Zero Framework was delayed following strong opposition from some Member States. A growing number of voices expressed skepticism over the speed, ambition, and potential impacts of the measure. It also became clear that several Member States who had supported the Framework in 2025 were reluctant to adopt the measure in the face of strong opposition. In particular, the prospect of using an explicit acceptance procedure would have overturned decades of precedent that has enabled the adoption of effective environmental regulations at the IMO. We also heard from states who emphasized the importance of action on GHG emissions, while also highlighting the potential harm to these states’ economies. By the time of the most recent meetings, it had become increasingly clear that support for the Net-Zero Framework was not enough for adoption in 2026.

Since the postponement, a key question had been whether opposition to the Net-Zero Framework reflected deeper resistance to any multilateral regulation of GHG emissions or was limited to specific elements of the approved draft framework. The answer began to emerge midway through the MEPC 84 session, when Member States who had opposed the Net-Zero Framework also stood by the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy and signaled a willingness to engage on a potential way forward. This signal was reinforced through agreement on a Terms of Reference negotiated on the floor between Member States who held divergent positions on several aspects of the regulation.

These meetings have left us in a constructive, if fragile, position. Member States are now preparing to find a way forward that can secure broad support while living up to the ambition of the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy. The significant progress made on guidelines – including GHG fuel intensity calculation, fuel certification, rewards for zero- and near-zero energy, onboard carbon capture, life-cycle assessment of fuels, and the tasks assigned to the independent group of experts (GESAMP) – points to a shared interest in moving the process forward. We also saw encouraging progress on the Energy Efficiency Framework, with a constructive debate on the metrics and enforcement mechanism. Success will now require Member States to approach future meetings with good faith and a willingness to find compromise: a spirit was evident throughout the recent meetings.

Bo Cerup-Simonsen,Chief Executive Officer

The Center will continue to support global measures that can meet the ambition of the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy, including its commitment to a just and equitable transition. As negotiations continue ahead of the next working group meeting in September, we will work to provide greater clarity for both industry and policymakers. Through our analytical tools and deep expertise in fuel and vessel technologies, we will support Member States in understanding the impacts of different measures and how they align with the goals of the Strategy. The coming months will be critical as Member States work toward a solution that is politically viable, technically robust, and aligned with the 2023 Strategy. Our focus will be on providing rigorous, data-driven analysis to support informed decision-making as the difficult process of policymaking continues.

“Countdown to IMO NZF” has always been part of a larger effort to communicate the Center’s insights on climate policy and the maritime industry in a digestible yet data-driven format. We will continue to show the world that a global agreement enabling a future of zero-carbon shipping is possible, and Countdown will continue to bring you the Center’s latest policy analysis and insights on regulatory developments at the IMO and elsewhere.— Bo Cerup-Simonsen, CEOSource: Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center
