Oil turns lower after Iran touts progress in Switzerland peace talks

2026-06-22 18:24

inOil & Companies News22/06/2026

Oil prices erased early gains and turned lower on Monday after Iran said good progress had been made in talks with the U.S. in Switzerland, even as U.S. President Donald Trump threatened fresh military action against Iran over Hezbollah attacks on Israel.

As of 02:18 ET (06:18 GMT), Brent Oil Futures expiring in August fell 2.1% to $78.92 per barrel, while West Texas Intermediate WTI crude futures edged down 0.4% to $76.30 per barrel.

Both contracts declined nearly 10% last week on optimism over the interim peace deal.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Aragchi said on Monday that “major progress” had been achieved during quadrilateral talks in Switzerland.

Technical discussions are set to continue this week at Switzerland’s Bürgenstock resort, the Qatari foreign ministry said. Negotiators also agreed on a framework to halt fighting in Lebanon and created a communications mechanism to facilitate the safe passage of commercial vessels through the strategically important waterway.

The discussions under a recently signed 60-day interim peace agreement, mediated by Qatar and Pakistan, brought together U.S. Vice President JD Vance and senior Iranian officials to discuss sanctions relief, maritime security, and a framework for future negotiations on Iran’s nuclear programme.

Tehran had announced on Saturday that it had again closed the Strait of Hormuz, citing continued Israeli military operations in Lebanon and alleged failures by Washington to uphold commitments linked to the interim accord.

“Iran must immediately stop their highly paid PROXIES in Lebanon from causing trouble. If they don’t, we’ll hit Iran very hard again, just like we did last week, only harder!!!” Trump said on Sunday in a social media post.

His comments underscored the fragility of the diplomatic process and prompted traders to rebuild a geopolitical risk premium into oil markets.

“Recent developments show that moving towards a more permanent deal will be challenging, with very real risks of a flare-up in hostilities during the 60-day ceasefire,” ING analysts said in a note.

Crude prices tumbled last week on expectations that the interim agreement would restore shipping through Hormuz and eventually allow more Iranian oil to reach global markets.

Investors are now closely watching whether negotiators can keep the peace process alive in the coming days.Source: Investing.com
