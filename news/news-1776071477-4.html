Iran war fuel crisis financially benefiting Russia and Vladimir Putin

Thu, 09 Apr 2026 00:58

Vladimir Putin appears to have benefited from the Iran war and the oil crisis.(Getty Images)

As the world grapples with the impact of what's being described as the biggest-ever shock to world energy markets, one winner has emerged from the mayhem: Russia's Vladimir Putin.

"The war in Iran is pretty good business for President Putin," Alexander Kolyandr from the Centre for European Policy Analysis told 7.30.

For more than three years, Russia's oil exports have faced sanctions, which have placed a cap on how much Moscow can charge for oil.

The sanctions, enforced by the G7, the EU and Australia, are aimed at limiting Moscow's earnings to fund its war in Ukraine.

Russian oil tankers have been subject to sanctions.(AP Photo)

But last month, with oil prices skyrocketing due to the Iran war and the near closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the US changed course.

It temporarily waived sanctions on Russian oil that had already been loaded onto tankers for export.

Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has slowed to a trickle during the war.(ABC News Graphics)

US Treasury secretary Scott Bessent described the move as a "short-term measure" intended to "promote stability in world energy markets".

Since the waiver was issued on March 12, extra revenue from Russia's oil exports has soared.

At the start of the Iran war in late February, Brent crude oil traded at about $US60 a barrel. Even after this week's ceasefire was announced by the US and Iran, the price remains at about $US100.

"The global price of oil is up and a very basic calculation gives you that if oil trades $US10 higher per barrel for a month, it gives the Russian budget $US1.6 billion," Mr Kolyandr said.

"So then if oil is $40 higher, make your calculation."

Scott Bessent said lifting sanctions on Russian oil that was already loaded onto tankers would "promote stability in world energy markets".(Reuters: Denis Balibouse)

In fact, former US Treasury Department adviser Roxanna Vigil says if the intent of waiving sanctions had been to help lower oil prices by providing more supply, it isn't working.

Sanctions on Iranian oil already loaded onto tankers were also lifted by the US, and both waivers are set to expire in the coming days.

US President Donald Trump will have to decide whether the waivers will be extended.

"It's going to be a hard decision because so far, from what we can tell, at least on the Russia side, Russia is benefiting," Ms Vigil told 7.30.

"The other reason why it's going to be a hard decision is because there is bipartisan criticism in the United States Congress."

Saul Kavonic says European nations have taken action against Russian oil.(ABC News: John Gunn)

Another consequence of lifting sanctions on Russian crude oil is the impact on the country's ability to ship oil to buyers.

"The United States actually freed a number of tankers," Mr Kolyandr said.

"They were somewhere there on the high seas filled with oil or refined products … they [were] just stuck there. Now if you free them and offload their oil, you can use them again."

EU sanctions against Russian oil are still in place. And energy analyst Saul Kavonic says while the world's attention has been on the Strait of Hormuz, European countries have been taking action on the high seas.

"Since the 27th of February when the war began, we have now seen European nations board or seize three Russian oil tankers. That is actually a major development," Mr Kavonic told 7.30.

"Europe has been very cognisant that the Iran war of itself, plus this waiver, is increasing the revenue that Putin is getting to help fund his war in Ukraine … Something you've seen since the war began was a big lift in efforts by both Ukraine and Europe to target Russia's oil exports."

Russia's Ust-Luga oil terminal complex was targeted in a strike.(Satellite image (c) 2026 Vantor)

Ukraine's response has been relentless drone strikes on Russian oil infrastructure. Attacks on export terminals and refineries knocked an estimated 40 per cent of export capacity offline last month. Russia says it has now significantly recovered.

Mr Kavonic says this war has shown again how interconnected the world's energy markets are — and how difficult they are to control.

"Some of them are quite hostile. And while we are in a position to tighten the screws with sanctions on one rogue nation at a time, it's very hard to do all of them at once."
