Iran is replenishing its missile and drone stockpiles during the ceasefire at a faster pace than before the war, an Iran Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) commander said today, despite US officials having said that much of Iran’s defense capability has been degraded.

“During the ceasefire period, our pace in upgrading and refilling missile and drone launch platforms is even faster than before the war,” Brigadier General Mousavi, commander of the IRGC Aerospace Force, said, according to Iran’s state broadcaster IRIB. “We know the enemy is unable to create similar conditions for itself and has to bring in munitions from across the world bit by bit,” he added, claiming that the US has lost control of the Strait of Hormuz, Lebanon and the region.

Earlier this month,Air Force Gen. Dan Caine saidthat US Central Command (CENTCOM) had “destroyed approximately 80% of Iran’s air defense systems, striking more than 1,500 air defense targets, more than 450 ballistic missile storage facilities, 800 one-way attack drones storage facilities.”

However, sources familiar with the intelligencepreviously told CNNthat roughly half of Iran’s missile launchers are still intact and thousands of one-way attack drones remain in Iran’s arsenal despite US and Israeli strikes, painting a different picture.

Here in Pakistan’s capital, preparations are underway for a possible second round of face-to-face talks over the coming days between the US and Iran to bring an end to the conflict.

Amid the uncertainty, Iran’s top negotiator and parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf gavea major interview to state media.

After he faced heavy criticism at home for giving too much away at the last round of talks, this was a chance to explain himself, and his comments were a primer for what his US counterparts may soon hear in person.

Overall he said “progress has been made”, that both sides had a “more realistic understanding of each other” but “the gaps remain significant”. Specifically, he cited nuclear issues as well as the Strait of Hormuz, the vital waterway which Iran wants to monetarize.

Ghalibaf accused US President Donald Trump of lying, saying Iran has not given up nuclear enrichment, and would not hand over the so called “nuclear dust” Trump insists the US will get hold of.

And while his television address was trying to sell unity and victory to the Iranian people, the signal to the US was clear — we have cards to play.

On the Strait of Hormuz, he said its rapid closure showed Iranian command and control remains robust, despite persistent American claims to have decimated Tehran’s military.

Ghalibaf’s speech had multiple messages aimed at different audiences, but one stood out from him — Iran has the strategic advantage and is prepared to keep fighting.

Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz continues to be paralyzed Sunday.

Some context:The Iranian announcement came in response to US President Donald Trump’s insistence that the US naval blockade of shipping using Iranian ports would continue – despite indications from Tehran that it was ready to loosen restrictions on shipping.

A narrow waterway that bypasses Iran and Oman, theStrait of Hormuzis the main route for shipping crude from oil-rich countries such as Saudi Arabia and Kuwait to the rest of the world.

Iran controls the strait’s northern side. About 20 million barrels of oil, or about one-fifth of daily global production, used to flow through the strait every day, according to the US Energy Information Administration, which calls the channel a “critical oil chokepoint.”

According to the EIA, “very few alternative options exist to move oil out of the strait if it is closed.”

The strait also carries about one-fifth of global trade in liquefied natural gas.

Energy analysts have warned that oil and natural gas prices are likely toremain elevateduntil the strait is passable.
