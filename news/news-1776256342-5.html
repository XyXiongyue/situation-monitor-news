What's The Rs 10 Lakh Crore Question Hanging Over US-Iran Talks

Wed, 15 Apr 2026 05:17

Iran has made a massive financial demand at the heart of ongoing negotiations with the United States, seeking the release of funds worth nearly Rs 10 lakh crore that remain frozen overseas. The issue has emerged as a key sticking point in discussions around sanctions relief, nuclear commitments, and broader diplomatic engagement. Decades of sanctions imposed by the US and its allies have restricted Iran’s access to global financial systems, leaving a substantial portion of its oil revenues and foreign exchange reserves locked in foreign accounts.

This demand has now become central to Tehran’s position in the latest round of talks, including recent discussions held in Islamabad.

Estimates suggest thatIranhas between $80 billion and $120 billion (roughly Rs 6.5 lakh crore to Rs 10 lakh crore) held abroad. While figures vary across reports, the total amount is widely believed to exceed $100 billion, according to several reports.

The U.S. is demanding a 20-year suspension of Iran's nuclear activities, while Iran is offering only a five-year pause. Additionally, the U.S. wants Iran to remove near-weapons-grade uranium and address other issues like the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and Iran's support for proxy groups. These divergent positions have created significant hurdles in the talks.

The origins of these frozen assets date back to sanctions introduced after the 1979 Islamic Revolution. However, the situation intensified after 2018, when then US President Donald Trump withdrew from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA). Although Iran briefly regained access to over $100 billion following the 2015 agreement, renewed restrictions once again blocked those funds.

Iran’s overseas funds are scattered across multiple countries, largely tied to oil trade and energy payments. A notable portion, around $6 billion, is currently held in Qatar. These funds were initially in South Korea but were moved as part of a 2023 prisoner exchange deal. However, their usage is restricted to humanitarian needs like food and medicine, and they were frozen again after the October 2023 Hamas-Israel conflict.

Another $10 billion is reportedly held in Iraq, linked to payments for Iranian gas and electricity exports. These funds remain inaccessible due to regulatory restrictions. Additionally, about $3 billion is tied up in Japan throughoil-related transactions.

Beyond these, Iranian assets are believed to be routed through financial networks in countries such as the UAE, China, Turkey, Singapore, India, and parts of Europe, often via indirect channels like shell entities and monitored accounts.

For Iran, reclaiming these funds is not just about economics; it is also about trust and negotiation power. The country is currently struggling with inflation exceeding 60 per cent, a weakening currency, and heightened geopolitical tensions, particularly around the Strait of Hormuz. Access to these funds could provide immediate economic relief and support essential imports and infrastructure spending.

At the same time, Iranian officials view the release of these assets as a test of credibility. Without concrete financial relief, Tehran remains wary of entering or committing to any fresh agreement.

Strategically, the demand also serves as a bargaining tool. Iran is linking the release of funds to broader issues, including its nuclear programme and regional security concerns, while the US is reportedly considering a phased approach tied to compliance measures.
