Trump, Vance Say Iran Agreed to Nuclear Inspections — Tehran Pushes Back

Mon, 22 Jun 2026 10:54

Iran has agreed to allow United Nations nuclear inspectors back into the country following two days of talks in Switzerland, US Vice President JD Vance said on Monday. Speaking to reporters in Lucerne before returning to Washington, Vance described the move as a significant development in negotiations between the two countries. "The Iranians have agreed to invite IAEA inspectors back into their country," he said.

"That is a major milestone for the American people and the first step in permanently denuclearizing, or permanently ending a nuclear weapons program in Iran, and that's exactly what we want to do. That's exactly what we asked to happen," the Vice President added.

Iran is demanding the lifting of long-standing US sanctions as part of the negotiations. The Iranian government seeks relief from economic sanctions that have significantly impacted its economy. This demand is central to the ongoing talks, as it is linked to Iran's willingness to limit its nuclear and missile programs in exchange for concessions from the US.

US President Donald Trump also welcomed thereported agreementin a post on Truth Social, writing: "Everybody is fully aware that Iran will agree to have Major Weapons Inspections in order to ensure 'Nuclear Honesty' long into the future."

However, Iranian officials appeared to dispute aspects of the US account. Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei told state-run IRNA that "Iran's interactions with the agency, in line with its obligations under safeguard agreements, will continue according to the existing framework."

Meanwhile, the semi-official Tasnim News Agency reported: "The issue of granting permission for IAEA inspectors to enter Iran was not approved by the Iranian negotiating team or by responsible government officials."

File photo showing Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian at an exhibition of Iran's nuclear achievements in Tehran in 2025.

Inspectors from the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) have entered Iran since last year's 12-day conflict between Israel and Iran.

However, Iran has continued to deny access to three nuclear facilities bombed by the United States on 21 June 2025, where nearly 1,000 pounds of highly enriched uranium is believed to remain buried.

The developments came as the Trump administration announced a temporary easing of restrictions on Iranian oil exports. US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said a new 60-day general licence would permit the production, delivery and sale of Iranian crude oil and petrochemical products through 21 August while negotiations continue.

"In line with the ongoing productive talks in Switzerland, Iran has committed to free and open transit in the Strait of Hormuz and to permit International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) inspectors into their country," Bessent wrote on X.

Vance said negotiators had made substantial progress during the talks. "We laid a very good foundation for a successful final deal," he said. "The final deal is the house. We set the foundation, we haven't built the house, but we've laid a successful foundation to get to a good place for the American people."

He also said the two sides had established "mechanisms" to address disputes concerning the Strait of Hormuz and the ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon.

Asked to elaborate, Vance said the mechanism involved "actually talking to each other and figuring out how to stop the shooting".

A joint statement issued by mediators from Pakistan and Qatar said a "de-confliction cell" involving the United States, Iran and Lebanon would be established to "ensure the adherence of the termination of military operations in Lebanon".

Vance also said that any Iranian assets unfrozen under a future peace agreement could be used to purchase American agricultural products. He credited President Trump's son-in-law, Jared Kushner, and Qatari officials with developing the proposal.

"That's a very, very good and very classic Trump deal that's great for our people, great for the people of Iran, and fundamentally, again, will contribute to this regional security architecture that we've built," he said.

The Vice President's remarks followed a series of comments made by President Trump on Sunday, including a threat to seize the Strait of Hormuz and "blow the s--t out of" Iran.

Vance said the remarks prompted concern among Iranian negotiators. "The comments by the president had caused the Iranians to threaten to walk out, or at least there were social media threats that they would walk out, but we were negotiating well past 1 in the morning yesterday, so they didn't walk out, and their technical team is still here," he said.

He dismissed suggestions that the comments had derailed negotiations. "What we told the Iranians yesterday is, 'When you guys engage in what us millennials might call trash talk, you can't expect the president of the United States not to respond and not to correct the record,'" Vance said. "So, yes, there was a little bit of threatening, there was a little bit of whining, but at the end of the day, the talks continued, and we made great progress."

A joint statement from Pakistan and Qatar said that while high-level discussions had concluded, technical negotiations would continue in Switzerland throughout the week, led on the US side by Jared Kushner and special envoy Steve Witkoff.

The talks are taking place under a 60-day interim agreement signed last week between the United States and Iran, aimed at ending more than three months of conflict and providing negotiators with a framework to address Iran's nuclear programme, frozen assets and other outstanding issues.
