US, Iran begin final peace talks after signing deal: Live war updates

Thu, 18 Jun 2026 13:54

Negotiations aimed at bringing a lasting peace between the U.S. and Iran have begun after top officials signed apactending the war on all fronts. But top U.S. officials maintain that military action will restart if Iran doesn't hold up its end of the deal.

“It takes two to tango,” Vice PresidentJD Vancesaid during a White House press briefing Thursday, when asked about a red line for the U.S. to return to military action. “If the Iranians want to change, great. We're going to help them. If they don't change, we still got all the cards.”

The memorandum of understanding signed electronically Wednesday kicks off negotiations over the most contentious issues dividing the two sides. Chief among them are whether Iran will abandon its nuclear program and whether the country will benefit financially from ending the war.

Negotiations between the United States and Iran “started today,” Vance said, adding that Iran would receive financial benefits and sanctions relief only if it complies with the peace agreement. He said he plans to go to Switzerland for negotiations, but the exact date hasn't been determined.

According to theagreement, the two sides have 60 days to negotiate outstanding issues. The agreement sets in motion changes aimed at restoring shipping through theStrait of Hormuz, including the lifting of the U.S. naval blockade, removal of American forces and demining of the critical waterway.

The pact comes more than 100 days after the United States and Israel launched the war on Feb. 28. Thousands have died in the fighting, including 13 U.S. service members. The war has rocked the global economy as the cost of fuel and other goods has risen precipitously.

After finally getting their hands on physical copies of the U.S.-Iran deal Thursday, Senate Republicans haven't exactly been rejoicing.

In fact, some are doing little to hide their skepticism – and, in some cases, outright frustration – with the agreement.

Sen. Roger Wicker, R-Mississippi, the powerful chairman of the Senate Armed Services Committee, said the proposed $300 billion economic development fund for the Islamic Republic would make similar payoffs under former President Barack Obama’s 2015 deal "look like a pittance by comparison."

"I am concerned that the memorandum of understanding negotiates away the victories of Operation Epic Fury in ways that are completely out of step with the President’s goals," he said in a statement.

Sen. Deb Fischer, R-Nebraska, said she wanted more details.

"I commend President Trump for taking decisive action with the goal of eliminating Iran’s nuclear weapons capability," she said in a statement. "After much progress, I’m urging the administration to not lose sight of that objective. The president has put us in a position of strength. We cannot afford to squander it."

Vance sounds off on Israeli critics of Trump's Iran deal

Vice President JD Vance slammed Israeli critics of President Donald Trump's memorandum of understanding with Iranafter a reportthat Israeli Prime MinisterBenjamin Netanyahuand top Israeli officials strongly oppose the agreement.

"The problem for Israel is not Donald J. Trump," Vance said during a Thursday briefing with reporters. "And anybody in Israel who thinks their biggest problem is the president of the United States needs to wake up and smell the reality of the situation that country is in."

The Israeli government was not shown the memorandum of understanding with Iran prior to Trumpsigning the documentWednesday.

Netanyahu has remained quiet publicly about the preliminary deal. But Israeli officials see the MOU as a strategic and political disaster,according to a report from Axios, one that falls significantly short of the "total victory" that Netanyahu promised in Iran.

The ceasefire in the MOU extends to a halt in fighting between Israel and Hezbollah, requiring Israel to withdraw from Lebanon under a final deal that Trump hopes to reach within 60 days. But Axios reported that Israel does not consider itself to be bound by the Lebanon portion of the agreement.

Vance, asked by a reporter about the criticism from the Israeli government, singled out Israeli Cabinet officials who have attacked the deal, saying Netanyahu has not shared the same opposition in the vice president's conversations with him.

On ballistic missile issue, Vance says Iran has a right to 'self-defense'

Asked whether the final agreement would restrict Iran’s ballistic missile program, Vice President JD Vance told reporters in the White House briefing room on June 18 that the U.S. does not expect the country to give up its right to self-defense.

“Israel doesn't give up the right of self-defense if Hezbollah fires rockets or drones at Israel. The Iranians don't give up the right of self-defense in their country,” he said.

However, Vance said the U.S. had been successful in destroying “a substantial number” of Iran’s ballistic missile launchers. In March, soon after joint U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran, Secretary of State Marco Rubio said that one of the purposes of the war was to destroy Iran’s missile capability.

On Thursday, Vance said what the Trump administration wants to see is that Iran is not funding regional instability, regional terrorism or trying to rebuild its nuclear weapons program.

“But we do expect that as part of the final deal, they're not going to be able to build the kinds of missiles that can broadly threaten the entire world,” he said.

Vance still plans Switzerland trip for Iran negotiations, probably this weekend

Vice President JD Vance said he will still go to Switzerland for negotiations on a final deal to end the war in Iran.

Vance was expected to travel to Geneva for a formal signing of the memorandum of understanding between the United States and Iran on Friday, June 19. However, President Donald Trump and Iranian leaders signed the deal remotely Wednesday.

The vice president, who is helping lead negotiations, said the White House plan is to still go to Switzerland.

“I don’t know exactly when,” Vance said when asked about the status of his trip. “We think these technical negotiations are going to start sometime this weekend. That’s still the plan, but that could change because it’s not an easy country – Iran – to get out of.”

When asked whether his trip could still happen Friday, Vance said he suspects it will happen this weekend.

“It just depends on exactly when the Iranians can get there,” Vance said.

Vance: 'Joy Behar is way tougher than the Iranians'

Vice President JD Vance on Thursday brushed off President Donald Trump's comments from the day before that the president planned to blame his vice president if the Iran deal goes off track.

"I think the president was joking, as he often does," Vance said at a White House press briefing when asked about Trump telling reporters that if the deal doesn't work out, then "I'm blaming JD."

Vance has served as the the Trump administration's lead negotiator with Iran and emerged as the face of the preliminary agreement Trump signed Wednesday.

"I think, look, the entire team has worked very well on this, and we've got this thing to a very good place for the American people," Vance said.

Vance added that he's heard some "progressive criticism," that he doesn't have experience in "hostile, high-stakes negotiations." He then referenced his recent appearance on ABC's "The View" amid his media tour touting the release of his new book.

"I would point those progressive critics to the fact that just two days ago, I spent over an hour on 'The View,' so I actually have a great experience in very hostile negotiations, and I've used that. I mean, look, Joy Behar is way tougher than the Iranians, and she and I are best friends now."

Congress to be briefed on Iran memorandum, Vance says

Vice President JD Vance said the White House plans to “brief Congress very soon” on the interim U.S.-Iran deal.

“I believe that they got the formal copy of the signed document this morning, and if not, they're going to get it at some point later today,” Vance said during a White House press briefing, adding that the administration is planning a briefing and has been informally briefing lawmakers over the past week.

Vance also said that he thinks the White House does not need congressional approval to temporarily lift oil sanctions, which is part of the memorandum with Iran.

“There are certain things that require congressional approval. There are certain things that don't require congressional approval. We feel quite confident that we can temporarily lift those sanctions without going to Congress and seeking their approval on that,” Vance said.

US 'not giving up a cent' to Iran, Vance says

Vice President JD Vance insisted that the United States "isn't giving up a cent of money to Iran" as he defended a $300 billion redevelopment fund for the country that's in a memorandum of understanding President Donald Trump signed with Iran.

Vance, appearing at a White House briefing with reporters, reiterated that Iran would only receive financial benefits and sanctions relief if it complies with the peace agreement.

Under the pact, the U.S. and its Middle East partners would "develop a definitive, mutually agreed plan with at least U.S.D. 300 billion for the reconstruction and economic development of the Islamic Republic of Iran," according to text of the agreement.

The mechanism for the fund's implementation would be finalized in a deal with Iran that the Trump administration hopes to complete in 60 days. The text does not make clear which countries ‒ or whether the private sector ‒ would be contributing to the fund.

"They need money to do anything," Vance said. "Their economy is in dire straits. But in order for them to get any integration into the world economy, they're going to have to show us and verify for us that they are changing their behavior."

60-day negotiations on final US-Iran deal begins today, Vance says

The 60-day negotiating period on a final peace deal between the United States and Iran began today, Vice President JD Vance said Thursday.

During a White House press briefing, Vance said the negotiations officially “started today,” noting that the memorandum of understanding was signed remotely.

What's the $300 billion redevelopment fund in Trump's Iran deal?

For years, President Donald Trump has slammed former President Barack Obama for effectively agreeing to transfer $1.7 billion to Iran as part of the 2015 nuclear deal with Iran that Trump abandoned.

But the memorandum of understanding Trump signed with Iran has raised questions about whether the U.S. could end up transferring a significantly greater sum to Iran to create a new Iranian redevelopment fund.

Under the pact, the U.S. and its Middle East partners would "develop a definitive, mutually agreed plan with at least U.S.D. 300 billion for the reconstruction and economic development of the Islamic Republic of Iran," according to a U.S. official who read the text of the agreement to reporters Wednesday on the condition of anonymity.

The mechanism for the fund's implementation would be finalized in a deal with Iran that the Trump administration hopes to complete in 60 days.

The U.S. official stressed that the provision does not obligate the U.S. to "ever pay a single cent to the Iranians." The official said that Iran would have to comply with terms in the peace deal to access the redevelopment fund.

The text does not make clear which countries ‒ or whether the private sector ‒ would be contributing to the fund. Trump, appearing at the G7 conference in France, denied that the U.S. would be paying into the fund. "We're not investing. We're not putting up 10 cents," Trump said. "People can decide to do that but that's up to them."

The president in his post also referenced “affordability,” which he calleda “fake word”during a press conference on Wednesday after he criticized Democrats who complain about rising prices due to the war.

A senior Trump administration official shared details of the  U.S.-Iran memorandum of understanding in a call on Wednesday. Some key points of the14-point plan include:

The deal calls for an end to hostilities on all fronts, including in Lebanon, as Israel has struck the country targeting Hezbollah. The deal will also reopen the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days, with the U.S. ending its blockade and Iran making efforts to ensure the "safe passage of commercial vessels with no charge."

U.S. forces within proximity of Iran will be removed "within 30 days after the final deal.”

In addition, the U.S. and regional partners will "develop a definitive, mutually agreed plan with at least USD 300 billion for the reconstruction and economic development of the Islamic Republic of Iran." The U.S. will also "terminate all types of sanctions" against Iran in "an agreed upon schedule as part of the final deal."

On concerns over nuclear, the memorandum says Iran "reaffirms that it shall not procure or develop nuclear weapons" and that the two countries agreed to "resolve the disposition of stockpile enriched material pursuant to a mechanism that will be mutually agreed upon."

Gas and oil prices fell June 18 the morning after U.S. and Iranian officials signed a memorandum of understanding aimed at ending the war.

Tehran appears hopeful for swift resolution of negotiations

Iran’s Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei said that the sooner the U.S. and Iran reach a conclusion in pending negotiations, "the happier we will be," according tostate media.

"From the time this agreement is signed, which essentially starts from now, we are supposed to hold talks over two issues for 60 days," Baghaei said. "As for the 60 days, the sooner we reach a conclusion, the happier we will be."

The two issues the spokesperson is referencing are Iran’s nuclear program and U.S. sanctions on Iran. The memorandum signed by leaders of the United States and Iran sets the stage for negotiations over the two issues.

According to the agreement, the two sides are supposed to negotiate the issues in 60 days but Baghaei said the deadline could be extended if necessary. However, according to state media, he did not think more time would be necessary.

"Since our negotiations have always been results-oriented and focused on outcomes, our effort is to reach a conclusion on these two issues in the shortest possible time," he said.
