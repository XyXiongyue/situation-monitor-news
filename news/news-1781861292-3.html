2 wars later, US-Iran nuclear diplomacy returns to square one

The attacks badly damaged Iran’s enrichment capacity but did not resolve the central proliferation concern: the location, status and future disposition of its near-weapons-grade highly enriched uranium (HEU) stockpile.

Now, after months of military escalation, analysts say the broad bargain on the table still resembles the one taking shape before the fighting began.

The MOU gives both sides 60 days to turn that bargain into a final deal, covering arrangements for Iran’s enriched uranium stockpile and future enrichment programme in exchange for sanctions relief, oil waivers, access to frozen assets and a proposed US$300 billion reconstruction and development plan.

According to Ludovica Castelli, project manager of the EU non-proliferation and disarmament consortium at the Institute of International Affairs in Rome, the MOU “appears to revive the core trade-off that was already emerging in the Oman channel” – constraints on Iran’s nuclear activities in exchange for sanctions relief, economic normalisation and recognition of limited civilian nuclear rights.

“The war altered the balance of leverage and sequencing, but not the fundamental architecture of the bargain,” Castelli said.

Every apparent concession to Iran in the MOU was, in fact, under discussion during negotiations last May, said Esfandyar Batmanghelidj, CEO of the Bourse and Bazaar Foundation, a London-based think tank.

American and Iranian negotiators had already discussed confidence-building measures, including oil waivers and the unfreezing of assets, he said.

The US$300 billion reconstruction fund outlined in the MOU was likewise originally envisioned as a regional investment vehicle, with capital coming from the Gulf monarchies.

“We shouldn’t criticise the MOU because it contains unreasonable concessions,” Batmanghelidj wrote in a social media post on Thursday. “We should be frustrated that the MOU contains very logical inducements and that the Trump administration was poised to offer these same inducements not just before the 40-day war, but also before the 12-day war last year.”

In his view, “we should consider ourselves fortunate that two wars later, Iran is still inclined to accept this deal, without dramatically different terms” – a reflection, he said, of “hard-nosed Iranian pragmatism and the underappreciated fact that the Iranian national security establishment takes the idea of diplomacy seriously”.

What remains is an Iranian nuclear programme in limbo, according to non-proliferation experts, after Iran’s industrial-scale enrichment capacity is believed to have been destroyed or rendered inoperable following attacks on its centrifuges and the plants where they were built.

Some facilities were struck again in March before a Pakistan-mediated truce took effect, though with relatively little additional impact, according to a satellite imagery study published by the Institute for Science and International Security on June 8.

Targets included the TESA Karaj centrifuge manufacturing plant, as disclosed in recent International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) verification reports. The UN nuclear watchdog also noted that Iran’s heavy water production plant and the Ardakan yellowcake facility were the only fresh nuclear programme targets during the now-frozen second phase of the conflict.

“There is no evidence of any uranium enrichment and the centrifuge programme remains destroyed,” the institute concluded.

Yet according to Daryl Kimball, executive director of the Washington-based Arms Control Association, the US and Israeli decisions to “illegally strike” Iran this year and last “set back the country’s nuclear programme, but military attacks cannot eliminate Tehran’s capability to build the bomb”.

Negotiating an effective, verifiable nuclear agreement that provided clear benefits to Iran “stands the best chance of disincentivising Tehran from developing nuclear weapons”, he said.

“The Trump administration cannot afford to squander – for the third time – this diplomatic opportunity,” Kimball warned.

He emphasised that Iran had “gained irreversible knowledge about the uranium fuel cycle” since Trump withdrew the US from the 2015 nuclear deal, knowledge that would allow Tehran to “quickly build back to the threshold of nuclear weapons” even if a final deal required complete dismantlement of its enrichment infrastructure.

Castelli agreed that a comprehensive deal “can constrain capabilities, but it cannot erase knowledge”, observing that Iran had accumulated “scientific expertise, engineering know-how and institutional memory over decades”.

At best, a robust agreement could push back the operational timeline for producing weapons-grade material by “several years”, but would not eliminate Tehran’s status as a “threshold nuclear state capable of rebuilding capacity if political constraints collapsed”.

The broad principles in the MOU were therefore “insufficient” on their own. “Without intrusive verification and rapid-access provisions, Iran would retain at least part of the latent capability required for a relatively swift breakout attempt,” Castelli said.

Non-proliferation experts broadly agree that Iran’s HEU stocks – including what remains of the 440.9kg (972lbs) of uranium enriched to near-weapons-grade 60 per cent U-235 that the IAEA estimated Iran held before the June 2025 strikes – should be downblended to under 5 per cent, the level sufficient for use in civilian power reactors.

Kimball said Iran should, under IAEA supervision, either ship the down-blended material to the international fuel bank in Kazakhstan or to Russia, sell it, or store it above ground under continuous monitoring. The 10 to 15-year suspension of uranium enrichment under negotiation could be “augmented with a freeze or verified limits” on centrifuge research and production, along with a prohibition or cap on uranium mining and milling.

Demanding permanent enrichment suspension “is not necessary for an effective agreement and would only drive Iran from the negotiating table”, Kimball said.

A verifiable enrichment suspension combined with dilution of the HEU stockpile would instead “provide much greater assurance that Tehran could not quickly dash to a bomb”, he added.

The final agreement should specify irreversible downblending procedures, quantitative caps on enrichment levels and stockpiles, real-time IAEA monitoring, challenge inspections at undeclared sites and an automatic sanctions snapback mechanism for non-compliance, Castelli said.

“Equally important is precise language” on centrifuge production, storage and reinstallation – details “often overlooked but central to breakout timelines”, she said.

Any sustainable agreement would “ultimately require strong political ownership from both sides”, Castelli added.
