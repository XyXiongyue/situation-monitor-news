Insulating PH from Mideast oil supply disruption is next to impossible

Wed, 15 Apr 2026 04:09

China, Russia and a few Southeast Asian neighbors produce oil but when push comes to shove, they will curtail their export sales …

The Philippines is a hard-luck country in terms of petroleum resources. It imports nearly all of its crude oil requirements, mostly from the Middle East, to fuel its transportation sector.

China, Russia and a few Southeast Asian neighbors produce oil but when push comes to shove, they will curtail their export sales to secure their energy security first.

The Strait of Hormuz lately has become the flashpoint and the cause of oil supply uncertainty. A fifth of the world’s crude supplies pass through this waterway and the US-Iran war has virtually closed the shipping lane.

The Philippines and the rest of Asian refiners, complains Jetti Petroleum Inc. president Leo Bellas, are struggling to secure prompt replacement crude cargos, “with some Chinese refiners already cutting runs and suspending fuel exports, resulting in a reduced available supply in the region.”

“Expectations of more refining run cuts could tighten fuel supplies even further,” he warns.

Energy Secretary Sharon S. Garin echoed the warning. The decision of China and the other countries to halt oil exports, she said, has drawn “a lot of concern” as they are suppliers of Philippine oil companies.

The Philippines in 2024 imported US$2.01-billion worth of petroleum gas, the 30th largest importer (out of 223) in the world, mainly sourced from China, Australia, Nigeria, Saudi Arabia and Brunei Darussalam.

The Middle East conflict, meanwhile, is already pointing to a slower gross domestic product (GDP) this year.

The World Bank just cut its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 3.7 percent from an earlier 5.3-percent estimate, as an energy shock sparked by Middle East conflict weighs on the region.

World Bank senior economist Ergys Islamaj said the downgrade reflects the country’s risk exposure to the war.

“[The] Philippines is exposed to [the] conflict not only through energy and fertilizer imports but also through remittances. Eighteen percent of remittances to the Philippines in 2025 came from the Gulf,” Islamaj said in an online briefing with reporters last week on the release of the bank’s East Asia and Pacific economic update.

“[A] longer conflict will hurt the economy further,” he said.

The bank’s Philippine downgrade aligns with the its projected East Asia and the Pacific growth slowdown to 4.2 percent in 2026 amid the energy shocks.

“The impact of the Middle East conflict depends on each country’s reliance on energy imports, existing vulnerabilities, and economic policy flexibility. Prolonged and intensified conflict may further increase economic distress and reduce regional growth,” the World Bank said.

The bank’s report identified the Philippines and Thailand as the economies most exposed to current volatility because of their heavy reliance on imported oil.

The Asian Development Bank (ADB) similarly trimmed its 2026 economic growth outlook for the Philippines to 4.4 percent from 5.3 percent as heightened risks from the ongoing Middle East conflict threaten energy supplies and remittance inflows.

In its Asian Development Outlook report released recently, the lender said growth would remain subdued this year because of the country’s high risk exposure to the region. The bank expects the economy to recover with a growth of 5.5 percent in 2027.

The ADB noted that the Philippines remains vulnerable to the conflict due to its heavy reliance on imported crude oil and refined products. The lender noted that transmission channels for the slowdown include potential disruptions in remittances from overseas Filipinos, tighter financial conditions and weaker investor sentiment.

If it’s any consolation, the conflict in Iran and other parts of the Middle East proves that the Philippine shift to renewable energy is a sound strategy.

It reduces our dependence on fossil fuel from volatile regions and secures the country’s energy supply. But that only helps in the local electricity market, which is currently supplied by solar, wind geothermal and hydro resources.

The road to electric transportation is promising but it still not on the horizon. That is the only way, though, to reduce our dependence on imported fuel and Middle East sources—and insulate us from geopolitical conflicts—unless, the Philippines hits the paydirt and discovers huge oil reserves beneath its seas.
