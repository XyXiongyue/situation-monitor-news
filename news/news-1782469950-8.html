Global base oils in for long recovery despite Hormuz reopening

2026-06-26 18:32

inOil & Companies News26/06/2026

Global base oils markets are facing a long recovery period as supply constraints and geopolitical tensions continue to reshape the industry, ICIS head of refining and base oils analytics Michael Connolly said on Thursday.

The situation is particularly acute for Group III base oils, where recovery timelines are significantly longer due to structural disruptions, including the major outage at the Pearl gas-to-liquids (GTL) facility in Qatar, Connolly told delegates at the ICIS Asian Base Oils & Lubricants Conference in Singapore.

“The longer that we go on with the conflict, the longer we take to recover,” Connolly said, referring to the Middle East war which started on 28 February and has impacted global base oil supply and logistics.

Under ICIS’ “extended scenario”, shipping flows through the Strait of Hormuz will see a beginning of normalization around the end of June or start of July.

Under this base case, the market expects a six- to nine-month period before achieving stabilization, meaning a return to normalcy is likely not until the end of 2026 or early 2027.

“There’ll be definite shortages for much longer in the Group III market, and that’s before we mention the quality of the material versus the new material that’s coming online,” he said.

Total Group III supply for the year is expected to be reduced by about 1.5 million tonnes, a “huge impact within the market”, he said.

While some China-based coal-to-liquids (CTL) plants have increased operating rates to help alleviate the shortage, “that isn’t the same quality material”, he warned.

The pace of supply recovery will ultimately drive the market trajectory, as supply recovery remains on the horizon, but normalization will take time even after the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz.

Although crude and gasoil economics remain relevant, the market direction is being steered by the overall supply and demand balance.

The recovery for Group III is expected to be particularly drawn out, characterized by significant structural damage and a lack of available substitutes.

Demand is being curtailed by a combination of price-led factors, where economics no longer work for blenders, and supply-led shortages where physical molecules are simply not available.

“Overall, the expected demand drop that we have for this year still will require a massive call on inventory,” he said.

The current environment brings significant implications for buyers, formulators, and blenders that necessitate a new strategic approach.

The industry must prepare for significant and sustained price pressure on all grades, with Group III facing critical shortages while Groups I and II are propped up by diesel markets.

Buyers in the Group III market facing limited-to-no spot availability must anticipate contract minimum commitments, and should expect additional volumes to be pricier.

Formulators are urged to look at reformulations now, while noting that engine performance level (EPL) waivers have time limits and the potential for disruption is long-term.

Strategic stocking is also becoming a priority to provide a buffer against potential further escalations or a multi-year Group III outage, with Europe currently emerging as the most attractive region.

“Rethinking about how you set your contracts up in the next few years is an important issue in that balance between contract and spot in those markets,” Connolly added.
