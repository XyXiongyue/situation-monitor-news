Oil soars past $100/barrel, drops peso to new low, hikes fuel prices

Fri, 24 Jul 2026 00:44

Escalating conflict in the Middle East rattled global financial markets on Friday, sending Asian stocks lower, pushing oil prices above $100 a barrel, weakening the Philippine peso to a record low, and raising expectations of another sharp increase in local fuel prices.Philippinesinvestment guide

Asian markets tracked a Wall Street selloff as investors reacted to a surge in crude prices, renewed concerns over inflation, and uncertainty surrounding the global economy.

The latest escalation came after the United States launched fresh strikes on Iran following attacks by Tehran-backed Houthi rebels on oil tankers in the Red Sea, opening a new front in the conflict and intensifying fears over global energy supplies.

Speaking on the sidelines of a Southeast Asian summit in Manila, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the Houthis had been “suckered” by Iran into attacking Red Sea shipping.

“The Houthis largely were smart and stayed out of all this throughout the conflict, but they now apparently have gotten themselves suckered into this, going after Saudi Arabia and their ships,” Rubio said.

Against the backdrop of escalating tensions, the Philippine peso fell to a fresh record low of 61.847 against the US dollar, weakening by 9.7 centavos from Thursday’s close of 61.75, according to the Bankers Association of the Philippines.Philippinesinvestment guide

The peso opened at 61.80 before slipping to an intraday low of 61.85.

Reyes Tacandong & Co. senior adviser Jonathan Ravelas said the peso weakened as the dollar strengthened on rising oil prices and renewed inflation concerns triggered by the conflict and fresh US tariffs.

“Expect the currency to range 61.70 [to] 62.00 levels in the near term,” Ravelas said.

Oil industry players also warned that the renewed hostilities are likely to trigger another round of substantial fuel price hikes next week.

“The news is fueling worries because prices are reacting to supply disruption concerns,” Jetti Petroleum president Leo Bellas said.

“With no clear sign of de-escalation in the Middle East conflict, the threat in the Red Sea has expanded the conflict beyond the Persian Gulf and will cause world oil prices to increase further.”

Fuel prices to rise by at least P6 per liter

Based on four-day trading in the Mean of Platts Singapore and foreign exchange movements, an industry source told Manila Standard diesel prices could rise by P6 to P6.50 per liter next week, while gasoline prices may increase by P5.50 to P6 per liter. Continued fighting could push adjustments to as much as P7 per liter.

The source said world oil prices have rebounded sharply as supply disruption concerns intensified, with tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remaining severely constrained and security risks spreading to the Bab el-Mandeb Strait.

“As the Gulf is a major source of diesel and tanker flows were only beginning to recover before hostilities flared again, renewed military strikes and a reciprocal blockade of the Strait of Hormuz by the US and Iran have raised fresh supply concerns,” the source said.

“Refinery disruptions and further constraints on Middle Eastern and Russian supply are keeping middle distillate supplies tight and prices elevated.”

The source added that gasoline prices have also strengthened due to tight global inventories, strong demand, and concerns over delayed recovery of Middle Eastern exports despite easing Chinese export restrictions.

Oil companies last week raised pump prices by P3.50 to P3.65 per liter for gasoline, P10 to P10.68 per liter for diesel, and P11.60 to P11.77 per liter for kerosene.

The latest adjustment brought the cumulative increase this year to P49.92 per liter for gasoline, P50.43 for diesel, and P48.39 for kerosene.

US: Hormuz strait still open for transit

The US military said the Strait of Hormuz, through which about one-fifth of the world’s oil previously passed, remained open for transit despite continued Iranian attacks on commercial shipping.

However, the Houthis’ attacks on vessels in the Red Sea heightened concerns over disruptions to crude exports.

International benchmark Brent crude climbed above $100 a barrel on Thursday for the first time since May after surging 8 percent and remained above that level on Friday.

President Donald Trump vowed to use frozen Iranian assets to compensate for damage to commercial shipping, a move Tehran warned would set an “incendiary precedent.”

The US military said it had completed its 13th consecutive night of strikes on Iranian military targets to “hold Iran accountable and diminish threats from the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps to commercial shipping.”

Iranian state media reported missile strikes on the southwestern city of Ahvaz, as well as explosions in Omidiyeh, Bandar Abbas and Qeshm Island near the Strait of Hormuz.

Trump said the United States would hold Iran responsible for the Houthis’ actions and warned that both would face a “major military punishment.”

United Nations Secretary-General Antonio Guterres appealed for restraint.

“The situation is getting out of control. It is teetering on the edge of the unimaginable,” Guterres told the UN Security Council.

“One crisis feeds another. One escalation triggers the next.”

European Central Bank President Christine Lagarde warned that “the energy shock could intensify further” and fuel inflation, while German Defense Minister Boris Pistorius cautioned that the conflict could have “dire consequences” for oil-importing economies.

The war resumed earlier this month after a brief ceasefire between the United States and Iran collapsed. Yemen’s Houthi rebels entered the conflict this week, declaring a blockade of Saudi ports and launching missile and drone attacks on commercial shipping.

“The armed forces’ retaliatory attacks will continue as long as the US attacks on the country’s infrastructure and coastal areas continue,” Iranian army spokesperson Mohammad Akraminia said.

The Houthis said they attacked two Saudi oil tankers, the Encelia and the Layla, in the Red Sea, with Saudi Arabia confirming that one vessel had been hit.—With Agence France Presse
