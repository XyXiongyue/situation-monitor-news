Opinion: B.C. should secure its place in global energy supply chains amid Middle East tensions

Tue, 14 Apr 2026 16:47

As the conflict in the Middle East enters its second month, a number of politicians and business leaders have viewed the war as an opportunity for Canada’s oil and gas industry.

Federal Energy and Natural Resources Minister Tim Hodgson stated in March 2026 at the CERAWeek energy conference in Houston, Texas: “Over the last two weeks, with Iran closing the Strait of Hormuz, it has truly never been clearer that oil and gas from reliable producers like Canada are what our allies need.”

There is some truth to this. In the short term, elevated liquefied natural gas (LNG) prices will benefit the province’s natural gas producers and LNG Canada, B.C.’s only large operating LNG exporter.

Looking beyond the headlines reveals a more complex global energy market being rapidly reshaped by what is becoming history’s largest oil and gas supply shock. Fed up with depending on oil and gas imports from the Gulf nations and an increasingly unstable America, Asian economies are doubling down on efforts to onshore their energy needs, and this means doubling down on clean energy.

Improving energy self-sufficiency for many economies is within reach today. Solar costs have declined 90 per cent since 2010 and onshore wind costs 70 per cent to a level where solar photovoltaics (PV) or wind now represent the cheapest available source of new electricity generation in most markets, according to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA). Globally, onshore wind costs are now 53 per cent cheaper than the lowest cost fossil fuel alternative, including coal. Renewables costs continue to fall year-over-year and are expected to be 35 per cent cheaper still by 2035, IRENA reported.

The long-running problem of intermittency with renewables—in other words when the wind doesn’t blow and the sun doesn’t shine—is also now being solved as battery costs have dropped below US$100 per kilowatt hour (kWh) for the first time, undercutting LNG electricity generation costs in nearly every global market.

The results are impressive around the world. In 2026, Pakistan’s solar boom is expected to save the country US$6.3 billion in imported fuel costs, reported the Secure Energy Project. Uruguay’s electricity grid is now 99 per cent powered by renewables at half the cost of using previously imported fossil fuels, according to Forbes. Ethiopia has banned the sale of new internal combustion cars to decouple its economy from oil imports, reported Electrek. And Japan’s restart of its nuclear programs and solar and option have reduced its LNG imports by almost 20 per cent since 2018, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA).

Fortunately, the province has taken some positive steps to seize this opportunity, but leaders must do more. Accelerating the approvals of critical mineral mines that have First Nations consent and equity partnerships is a no-brainer. B.C. is Canada’s largest producer of copper, the only producer of molybdenum and also produces zinc and manganese. In addition, B.C. could also produce nickel, rare earth elements, niobium, tantalum, tungsten and cobalt—all inputs into clean energy technologies. The brass ring for B.C. is not just to mine these elements for export as raw materials, but to process them here for use in Canadian clean energy supply chains.

Generating more clean electricity is also a no regrets action. B.C.’s electricity grid is the backbone of its economy whileB.C.’s competitive advantageis its third lowest electricity rates in North America. Foreign investors are increasingly looking for affordable, clean and reliable electricity systems. B.C. is well-positioned to attract these investments, as well as export clean energy in the form of electricity to the United States and green ammonia to Asia. BC Hydro’s recent clean power calls are a good start, but this must be accompanied by permitting reforms to get projects built.

Lastly, the province must continue to support B.C.’s clean tech industries. B.C. is home to nearly500 clean energy companies, including four of the top 100 global clean tech companies—CO280 Solutions Inc., Mangrove Lithium, Moment Energy Inc. and Pani Energy Inc.—with85 per cent growthin the sector since 2021.

Energy markets are heading in one direction and that direction is electrified. While LNG exports may make the headlines, B.C. must not overlook the actual economics of energy—economies are fuel-switching from fossil fuels to electricity to improve energy security while realizing cost savings. B.C. is best positioned to help our allies and trading partners on their journeys toward energy sovereignty via clean energy—the type of energy sovereignty that will last.

Mark Zacharias is a fellow at the Centre for Global Studies and Pacific Institute for Climate Solutions at the University of Victoria. Joanna Kyriazis is the director of policy and strategy at Clean Energy Canada, a think tank at Simon Fraser University.
