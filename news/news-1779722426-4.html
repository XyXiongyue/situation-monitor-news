Iran seeks $12 billion frozen funds in Qatar released before advancing US talks: Report

Mon, 25 May 2026 07:30

Iran is demanding the release of $12 billion in frozen assets held in Qatar before moving ahead with negotiations with the US, according to reports, as talks over a possible ceasefire, sanctions relief and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz continue.

Iran is demanding the immediate release of $12 billion in frozen assets held in Qatar as a condition for moving forward with negotiations with the US, according to a report by Iran International citing sources familiar with the discussions.

Sources cited in the report said the $12 billion represents only an initial tranche sought by Tehran to begin the proposed diplomatic roadmap and does not reflect the full amount Iran wants released globally as part of a broader agreement.

Iran has reportedly insisted that all of its frozen assets worldwide must eventually be unfrozen and made fully accessible under any final deal with Washington.

Iran’s IRGC-linked Tasnim News Agency reported that disagreements between Tehran and Washington over one or two clauses in a proposed memorandum of understanding (MoU) still remain unresolved.

According to Tasnim, Iran wants the initial agreement with the US to include the release of at least part of its frozen funds before any final negotiations begin. Tehran is also demanding guarantees that the released money can be accessed freely.

The report added that the US is attempting to link the release of Iranian assets to a final nuclear agreement, while Tehran is pushing for partial sanctions relief and access to funds before signing a broader deal.

Tasnim also warned that disagreements over frozen assets could still derail the negotiations entirely.

One of the key elements of the proposed agreement is the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, a critical global oil shipping route.

According to an Axios report, the draft MoU states that the strait would remain open without tolls and that Iran would remove naval mines reportedly deployed in the area.

In return, the US would ease restrictions on Iranian ports, lift parts of its naval blockade and waive certain sanctions, allowing Iran to resume oil exports more freely.

Reports suggest that discussions over Iran’s nuclear programme and its stockpile of enriched uranium would only take place during the final phase of negotiations after the MoU and a proposed 60-day ceasefire are agreed upon.

However, the draft agreement would reportedly require Iran to halt any pursuit of nuclear weapons.

Iranian media claimed the initial peace framework would include US sanctions relief on Iranian oil exports and mutual commitments by both sides not to target each other or their allies.

Iran’s Central Bank chief Abdolnaser Hemmati travelled to Qatar on Monday following recent discussions in Tehran with a Qatari delegation over Iran’s frozen funds.

Meanwhile, Iranian Parliament Speaker and chief negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived in Doha for talks with Qatar’s Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani.

Officials said the meetings are focused on discussing the terms of a possible ceasefire agreement between Iran and the US.
