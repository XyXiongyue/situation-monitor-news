Oil prices sink amid U.S.-Iran negotiations, Hormuz deal hopes

2026-05-27 18:22

inOil & Companies News27/05/2026

Oil prices fell on Wednesday as negotiations between the U.S. and Iran continued despite reports of an exchange of fire this week.

Crude retreated after rising sharply in the prior session after the U.S. said it had attacked Iranian targets, drawing threats of retaliation from Tehran.

Brent oil futures for July, the global oil benchmark, declined 3.2% to $96.41 a barrel by 05:06 ET (09:06 GMT), while U.S. West Texas Intermediate crude futures dropped 4.2% to $89.88 a barrel.

“Oil prices are under pressure […] amid shifting expectations around a potential US‑Iran deal, although risks remain elevated with ongoing tension near the Strait of Hormuz,” analysts at ING said.

U.S.-Iran talks continue despite renewed military action – reportAl Jazeera reported that indirect negotiations between the U.S. and Iran continued despite an exchange of fire earlier this week.

While a tenuous ceasefire also remained in place, the report indicated that a peace deal appeared distant after this week’s hostilities.

U.S. officials had also signaled on Tuesday that a deal with Iran– to end the war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz– remained days away.

Iranian media reports said the country had fired upon an American drone and a fighter jet after the U.S. military said it had conducted strikes against missile launch sites and mine-laying boats in Southern Iran.

Tehran had decried the attacks and warned of retaliation if the U.S. violated their ceasefire.

The U.S. military claimed that the strikes were “defensive,” and that the ceasefire with Iran remained in place.

Reports earlier this week said the U.S. and Iran were close to a framework deal to end their war. The terms of the deal included an extension in their ceasefire and a reopening of Hormuz.

The deal will also see Iran enter into negotiations over its nuclear activities. Tehran has so far largely rejected U.S. demands to relinquish its enriched uranium stockpiles.

Hormuz remained a key point of focus in the war, with Iran having effectively blocked the waterway after the onset of U.S. and Israeli hostilities.

Hormuz’s closure blocked about a fifth of global oil supplies, severely crimping markets.

But reports that a few vessels had successfully crossed the channel this week helped spur some optimism over its reopening.

Still, oil flows through Hormuz remained at a fraction of pre-war levels.Source: Investing.com
