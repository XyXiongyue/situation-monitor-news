USFR: Ultrashort-Dated Floating-Rate Treasuries For Liquidity

Mon, 20 Apr 2026 14:43

WisdomTree Floating Rate Treasury Fund ETF (USFR) offers exposure to short-dated, floating-rate US Treasuries, closely tracking Federal Reserve rate policy.

USFR is most attractive during rate-hiking cycles, as coupon payments adjust upward with rising rates, providing room for a more robust distribution yield.

The ETF features high liquidity, minimal credit risk, a low 0.15% expense ratio, and an effective duration of just 0.02 years.

Investors should monitor Fed policy closely, as USFR distributions can fluctuate significantly with rate cuts or hikes.

The WisdomTree Floating Rate Treasury Fund ETF (USFR) is a fixed-income exchange-traded fund designed to provide investors with exposure to short-dated, floating-rate US Treasuries. With the short time to maturity and floating rate feature of the fund, investors will be fully exposed to Federal Reserve interest rate policy and the market’s expectations towards rate cuts or hikes. With uncertainty growing in the markets regarding the Federal Reserve’s rate policy for 2026 following a dramatic rise in oil prices, USFR can be a candidate for investors who believe that the Fed will hike rates in their attempt to fend off inflationary pressures, though may face a decline if the rate-cutting cycle were to continue. Ideally, investors may seek to utilize USFR when the key rate is expected to be hiked while using a fixed-rate strategy for rate cuts.

About WisdomTree Floating Rate Treasury Fund ETF

USFRwas launched on February 4, 2014 on the NYSE Arca Exchange. The ETF has a net expense ratio of 15bps, aligned with peer short-term Treasury strategies on the market. USFR exhibits substantial liquidity with $17.17 billion in assets with an average of $260 million in share value changing hands on a daily basis at the time of writing.

The ETF pays out a monthly distribution based on the auction spread and interest income from the floating-rate notes. The distribution over the last 12 months summed to $2.02/share, yielding 4%. Given that the strategy holds FRNs, distributions can vary drastically from year to year depending on the Federal Reserve’s interest rate policy and the economic cycle. During zero interest rate policy [ZIRP], the distribution was effectively cut given the yield curve tapering as held by the Fed at the time.

Resulting from the rate hike cycle in recent years, the distribution returned, peaking in 2023/2024, before falling back in 2025 following the end of the rate-hiking cycle and the beginning of the rate-cutting cycle.

Essentially, USFR will nearly fully reflect the Fed’s interest rate policy as the “floating” aspect of the interest rate will adjust in accordance with the target range. In terms of the auction spread for new issuances, the spread tends to widen during a period of interest rate cuts while tightening during interest rate hikes. The rationale behind this is that FRNs become more valuable if interest rates are hiked due to the adjusted interest rate tied to the notes. As opposed to fixed-rate notes, which exhibit an inverse correlation to interest rate policy, floating-rate notes exhibit more positive correlation as a result of theappreciatingvalue of the coupon during rate hikes.

Factors to Consider Before Investing in USFR

USFR can be considered as a macro strategy in which investors will base their investment decision on the direction of the Fed’s interest rate policy. Factors worth considering include inflation, GDP growth, and employment. Interest rate policy is the first tool in the Fed’s arsenal deployed to fend from heightened inflationary pressures, as exhibited throughout 2022-2023. When the market virtually collapsed in 2020 due to liquidity lockup and a halt in economic activity, the Fed cut the key rate to ZIRP, providing easy financing to companies in need.

Following the pandemic lockdowns, inflation became a challenge, resulting in drastic rate increases in 50bps increments. This effectively tightened borrowing costs in an attempt to push down the rate of inflation.

2026 started with optimism regarding the moderating rate of inflation before the war of Iran hit the oil markets, sending oil prices as high as $112/bbl before falling back on early signs of a peace agreement. As a result of higher oil prices, headline inflation jumped in for the month of March 2026 after multiple months of sequential decline. As a result of this increase in inflation, the Fed has been on the sidelines, looking for an indication as to the direction of the market and how things will play out. With oil futures coming off their highs, the market may be correcting to a more normalized environment.

One useful tool for investors to monitor is theFedWatch tooloffered by CME Group, Inc. (CME). The tool can be utilized to evaluate the market’s expectation of interest rate policy changes by month. While this shouldn’t be used to forecast interest rate policy, it can help the investor understand the pricing mechanisms for FRNs and help understand how the market perceives future rate policy. For example, there is a 66% probability that rates will remain unchanged through September 2026 and an emerging change that the rate may be cut by 25bps.

USFR can be used by a variety of investors seeking liquidity and exposure to short-dated FRNs. The strategy can be used for liquidity given its high trading volumes and depth. USFR can be used to park unallocated funds or as a component of an investor’s fixed income strategy. USFR can be best utilized during rate-hiking cycles given the adjusted coupon rate on the FRNs.

USFR directly exposes investors’ interest rate policy set by the Federal Reserve, meaning that distributions may adjust up or down when interest rates are hiked or cut. This can, in turn, influence cash flows received while holding this strategy throughout the rate cycle. USFR exhibits next to no credit risk with notes backed by the full faith of the US Government. USFR has an effective duration of 0.02 years, meaning that the price of the notes will exhibit minimal pricing adjustments relating to Fed interest rate policy.
