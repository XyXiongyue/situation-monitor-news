The market staged an impressive late-week recovery to finish on a significantly firmer footing, with improving sentiment driven primarily by renewed strength in the Pacific. Having come under pressure through the early part of the week, the market reversed course, with the BCI 182 5TC climbing sharply to close at $37,631, up from $36,756 at the start of the week. The turnaround was led by a resurgent C5 market, where rates rallied from around $10.80 on Wednesday to fixtures being concluded at $12.50 by Friday, supported by healthy cargo volumes, sustained miner activity, and a more confident owner stance. The Atlantic remained the weaker basin for much of the week, with limited fresh enquiry and a wide gap between charterers’ bids and owners’ expectations restricting trading activity. However, conditions improved into the close, with sentiment becoming noticeably more constructive. While fixtures remained limited, C3 bids strengthened to around $31.00, with owners holding firm in the $32.50-$33.00 range, suggesting the market may finally be finding a floor after several sessions of heavy pressure. Although an increasing number of ballasters heading west continues to temper the medium-term outlook, the stronger finish on both sides of the market leaves sentiment entering the new week on a considerably more positive note.

The week saw a clear divergence between Atlantic and Pacific markets. In the Atlantic, sentiment remained positive despite fluctuating activity, with an 81,000-dwt fixing for a transatlantic round at $21,000 and another 83,000-dwt at $20,750. Tight prompt tonnage in the North Continent and West Mediterranean supported rates, with some fixtures achieving premiums for earlier windows, such as an 85,000- dwt fixing for $22,500 for a transatlantic round. Overall, supply and demand appeared balanced, with steady enquiry for both transatlantic and fronthaul routes. Conversely, the Pacific market weakened. Limited cargo volumes, subdued demand from Australia and the North Pacific, and a growing surplus of prompt tonnage pressured rates, with an 82,000-dwt fixing a North Pacific round at $21,750 early in the week falling to around $17,000 later. Period activity was seen early on, with an 82,000-dwt fixing for one year at $17,800, alongside shorter cover including a 96,000-dwt fixing for two laden legs at $23,250 and a 76,000- dwt fixing at $18,250.

The Index climbed on a daily basis this week driven by the strengthening Atlantic market, whilst the Asian market remained fairly static. North America was very active especially on early positions as Charterers looked to cover prior to the upcoming holidays with 63,000-dwts fixing at around $32,000 for trips to the Far East and similar vessels fixed into the Mediterranean at around $33,000.The Continent saw pockets of activity with a 63,000-dwt fixed for scrap at $25,750 to the East Mediterranean whilst the East Mediterranean market itself saw consistent strong demand throughout the week including very strong fronthaul fixtures of $24,500/$25,000 delivery East Mediterranean for trips via EC South America to the Far East. Asia was more subdued although rates largely remained unchanged with a 64,000- dwt covered at $25,000 delivery CJK for a trip to West Africa, and a large number of fixtures reported from South Africa at the start of the week. Period interest remained and there was a report of a 64,000-dwt new building delivery ex yard November/December being fixed at $17,500 for threeyears.

Overall, the Handy market trended firmer over the week, supported by continued gains in the South Atlantic and US Gulf, while the Continent and Mediterranean remained steady and Asia stayed broadly balanced. In the Continent and Mediterranean, activity remained relatively muted, with charterers keeping bids close to last-done levels; a 37,000- dwt vessel was reported fixed from the Baltic to the West Mediterranean at $13,500. The South Atlantic and US Gulf continued to firm, supported by stronger bids and improving confidence. A 35,000-dwt was reported fixed from Recalada to Fortaleza at $25,000, while a 36,000-dwt open Puerto Cabello on 20-21 June was placed on subjects from SW Pass to the UK-Continent at $23,500. Asia remained broadly balanced, with fixtures including a 40,000-dwt fixing two laden legs in the $19,000s and a 31,000-dwt placed on subjects from North Vietnam to the Arabian Gulf in the $20,000s. Period activity also emerged, with a 40,000-dwt vessel fixed from Coatzacoalcos for 4 to 6 months at $18,500.

Market Segment Summaries•VLCC:The global market experienced a highly charged, event-driven week as accelerating peace deal negotiations ignited fierce bullish optimism among owners. A severe lack of operational transparency continues to cloud proceedings, with intense under-the-radar fixing creating a market where “the map does not fit the terrain.” In the Middle East Gulf (MEG), volumes transiting the Strait of Hormuz remained limited and mostly drawn from existing inventories. However, anticipation of an imminent reopening caused owners to aggressively hold back tonnage to wait for lucrative inside-AG business. The few available vessels willing to work drove a massive rate rally: GOO/China benchmarks surged from the WS 150 level to close at WS 250 on subjects, with prompt listings early this week already touching WS 260. Atlantic basin rates climbed sharply off the back of this Eastern momentum. Sustained export volumes out of Brazil acted as a vital engine for the basin, compensating for muted Asian appetite for WTI and West African grades; consequently, Brazil/China rates skyrocketed from WS 150 up to WS 197. While the U.S. Gulf saw minimal concluded activity alongside a string of failed fixtures, the tightening prompt list and rumors of a massive $22M target for USG/East indicate that the region is poised to follow the wider Atlantic upward curve.

•Suezmax:The market surged back onto an upward trajectory, heavily propelled by the massive bullish momentum spilling over from the VLCC sector and a rapidly tightening global position list. In West Africa (WAF), a late-week clearing of the tonnage list left multiple cargoes outstanding; owners capitalized on the scarcity to push rates aggressively, driving TD20 expectations into the WS 170–185+ range (with East-bound runs targeting WS 200). Tonnage availability remains incredibly thin across both the Atlantic basin and for units committed via the Cape of Good Hope. The Mediterranean CPC market saw rates blast through the WS 250 barrier, while long-haul Libya/Ningbo runs via Cape are now being assessed up near $9.0M. In the Americas, strong regional enquiry out of Brazil and Guyana (both hovering at WS 175+), combined with charterers splitting VLCC stems into Suezmaxes, severely constrained local availability and pushed USG-UKC up to WS 145. Meanwhile, the Persian Gulf market is highly charged but physically hanging in the balance; while owners are repositioning tonnage to the region and pushing ideas toward WS 220 for MAF-Singapore, sentiment is split between those betting on an imminent Strait of Hormuz reopening and more bearish players tracking ongoing regional conflicts.

•Aframax:The global market showed a sharp geographic divide, with Europe and the U.S. facing sustained downward pressure while the Asia-Pacific basin entered a firmer territory. In the Mediterranean, an oversupply of tonnage coupled with thin enquiry allowed charterers to aggressively chip away at rates, dragging cross-Med benchmarks down to WS 185 and pushing Ceyhan loaders below WS 190. The North Sea also suffered a negative swing, settling at a new conference level of WS 140 on longer Baltic voyages due to thin fixture counts forcing local players into a race to the bottom, though a trimmed position list and steady westbound ballasting by transatlantic players are lending near-term stability. The U.S. Gulf market fell significantly before finding a late floor, repeating WS 165 for TD25 runs as an afternoon demand flurry helped offset a heavily populated prompt list. Conversely, the Indo-East region provided a robust bright spot; fueled by steady regional demand and a tightening position list, TD14 gained 8 points over the week to push Indo/Oz rates up to WS 150, pushing Time Charter Equivalent (TCE) earnings to just under $30,000/day

•Product Tankers:The sector faced downward pressure across most regions as the ongoing displacement of Eastern-routed tonnage and the re-entry of Russian-trading units into conventional, nonRussian markets heavily populated lists. In the Middle East, ongoing regional attacks prolonged owner frustration, though LR2s saw a modest, slow uptick in activity with traders eagerly preparing for a scheduled Iran-U.S. MOU signing. LR1s traded flat with paper markets inching up, while Middle East MRs experienced another difficult week; a severe lack of open cargoes ex-Sikka drove TC17 rates down from WS 250 to WS 250–270, and down to WS 250 by the weekend. In the Atlantic basin, oversupply and a crashed U.S. Gulf market began driving a resupply of ships back toward Europe. Northwest Europe MR rates held steady for the time being with TC2 at WS 145 (and ARA-WAF at WS 205), despite an increasingly lengthy position list. Meanwhile, the Mediterranean MR market proved highly challenging; a severe tonnage overhang and weakening adjacent Handy rates eroded owners’ ideas, dragging cross-Med rates down to WS 195 and pinning Med-TA to WS 145 as owners struggle to find profitable outlets.Source: Norne Research
