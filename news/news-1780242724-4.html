Tehran insists economic relief and security guarantees must precede nuclear concessions as fragile US–Iran negotiations continue amid deep mistrust and competing strategic pressures.

Iran’s chief negotiator said on Sunday that Tehran would not agree to any deal with the United States unless it fully secures Iranian rights as a White House meeting chaired by President Donald Trump on Friday ended without a final decision on a potential agreement. Reports suggest the talks have stalled over a tougher peace proposal that Washington has sent back to Tehran.

Any tweaks to the proposal could further delay an agreement to formally end the West Asia war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz after weeks of fraught negotiations marked by sharp rhetoric and occasional flare-ups of violence.

The statement comes amid renewed diplomatic activity between the two sides including discussions around a temporary ceasefire framework, maritime security in the Strait of Hormuz, and limits on Iran’s nuclear programme. But despite reports of incremental progress, Iranian officials continue to caution that no final understanding has been reached.

Iran views sanction relief and the release of its assets frozen in banks abroad as among its key rights to be ensured under any deal with the United States.

Since the outbreak of the war, Iran has kept tight control over the strategic Strait of Hormuz, a vital global energy conduit, and sees oversight of shipping through the waterway as within its rights.

Negotiations deepen, but trust deficit widens

The US and Iran are reportedly exploring a phased arrangement that could extend the current ceasefire and reopen key shipping routes through the Strait of Hormuz, a critical artery for global oil flows. Washington has also pushed for stricter controls on Iran’s enriched uranium stockpile and greater transparency in nuclear activities.

However, Iranian officials insist that past experiences have eroded trust in US commitments. Tehran argues that economic relief including sanctions easing and access to frozen assets abroad — must be front-loaded and guaranteed before any long-term nuclear concessions are made.

Ghalibaf’s comments also reflect Iran’s internal political dynamics, where hardliners increasingly frame negotiations through the lens of leverage rather than compromise. Some Iranian leaders have openly argued that past concessions were secured through “pressure and resistance” rather than diplomacy, reinforcing scepticism toward US intentions.

Analysts say this position complicates efforts to translate a fragile ceasefire into a durable agreement. While Washington views phased negotiations as a pathway to stability, Tehran appears to be demanding immediate, measurable gains before committing to deeper structural changes.

US political constraints add pressure to fragile talks

On the American side, President Donald Trump is navigating competing pressures — balancing the economic incentive of stabilising global oil markets with domestic political demands for a hard line on Iran’s nuclear programme.

Reports suggest Washington is open to extending the ceasefire and advancing a framework agreement, but senior officials have also warned that military options remain on the table if negotiations collapse. This dual-track approach — diplomacy paired with deterrence — has become a defining feature of the current US strategy.

Yet unresolved issues continue to stall progress. These include Iran’s stockpile of highly enriched uranium, the sequencing of sanctions relief, and the long-term status of maritime access through the Strait of Hormuz. Each side is seeking leverage without appearing to concede strategic ground.

Experts say this creates a structural deadlock: Iran wants guaranteed economic relief before making irreversible nuclear commitments, while the US insists on verifiable restrictions before easing pressure.

Outlook: fragile diplomacy under strategic mistrust

Despite ongoing engagement, both sides remain far from a final agreement. The current phase of talks appears focused on managing escalation rather than resolving core disputes.

Iran’s insistence on “tangible results” highlights the central contradiction in the negotiations: diplomacy is advancing, but trust is not. Unless both sides bridge this gap, analysts warn that even a temporary ceasefire may remain vulnerable to political shocks and renewed escalation.

For now, the process continues but under the shadow of unresolved mistrust and competing strategic imperatives that have defined US–Iran relations for decades.
