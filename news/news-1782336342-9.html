Oil falls as more supply flows through Hormuz; Brent crude at below $77/bbl

2026-06-25 05:25

inOil & Companies News25/06/2026

Oil prices were extending losses on Wednesday, with Brent crude easing below $77/barrel, amid signs that more tankers stranded in the Persian Gulf are set to cross the Strait of Hormuz as USIran peace talks advance.

At 04:20 GMT, both Brent and US WTI crude benchmarks were trading lower by around 0.6%

Oil prices have fallen this week on easing supply concerns, after the US granted Iran a 60-day sanctions waiver following initial peace talks, allowing the Middle Eastern country to sell oil; and as hostilities in Lebanon eased.

“While key issues, including Iran’s nuclear program, the Lebanon ceasefire, and the full reopening of Hormuz, remain unresolved, the market is increasingly pricing in a normalisation of regional energy flows,” MUFG Research analyst Soojin Kim said.

“Oil prices are likely to remain under pressure as supply recovers and risk premiums continue to unwind.”

The market is also pricing in the return of Venezuelan crude following a series of US licenses that permit companies to negotiate and expand energy projects in the Latin American country.Around 1.2 million barrels/day of Venezuelan crude is now reaching the markets, “helping to plug the gap of lost heavy sour oil”, according to ICIS head of energy and refining Ajay Parmar.

HORMUZ OIL FLOWS RISING

Seven tankers, including two fully laden non-Iranian supertankers, openly crossed the Strait of Hormuz on 23 June with their satellite signals switched on, according to newswire agency Bloomberg; while three stranded supertankers passed through the crucial waterway on the same day, according to another newswire agency Reuters.

Iran’s semi-official Fars news ⁠agency on 23 June said that a limited number of vessels are being allowed to pass through the strait each day under coordination with Tehran’s Revolutionary Guards Navy.

The growing number of crude tankers sailing through the strait points to growing confidence among shipowners and traders regarding regional security.

The International Maritime Organization (IMO) on 23 June said it will start implementing an evacuation plan for over 11,000 seafarers stranded in the region to sail through the strait after the US-Iran ceasefire deal.

Oman has coordinated with the IMO to make available a temporary maritime corridor for all vessels, in accordance with the coordinates announced by the IMO, the country’s defense ministry said on 23 June.

Iran and Oman have also opened talks for a joint Hormuz transit framework, including possible passage fees via a joint committee, the Islamic Republic News Agency (IRNA) reported on 23 June.

Adding to supply-side relief, the UAE has restored most of its oil export capacity disrupted by the Iran conflict, with shipments in early June recovering to nearly 85% of pre-war levels, according to a report by the International Energy Agency (IEA).

The UAE exported about 4.3 million barrels of oil per day in early June, more than double the March volumes of around 1.9 million barrels/day.

Meanwhile, Kuwait is taking steps to normalize exports following the US-Iran peace framework, with Kuwait Petroleum Corp offering naphtha cargoes for direct pick-up from ports inside the Persian Gulf, requiring transit through the Strait of Hormuz, according to MUFG Research’s Kim.

“The move, alongside the lifting of force majeure notices and plans to increase oil production, signals growing confidence in the gradual recovery of regional energy flows,” he said.Separately, Russia is weighing a ban on diesel exports to avoid domestic shortages following Ukrainian drone strikes on its refineries, according to media reports.
