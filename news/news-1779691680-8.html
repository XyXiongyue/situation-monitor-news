Hope for lower fuel prices as US and Iran edge closer to deal

Sun, 24 May 2026 23:39

South Africans worried about rising fuel prices may finally have a reason for cautious optimism after the United States and Iran signalled progress in negotiations aimed at ending the war.

Global oil prices dipped after reports emerged that both countries were moving closer to a framework agreement that could help reopen the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world’s most important oil shipping routes.

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow passage between Iran and Oman through which a significant portion of the world’s oil supply travels.

Any threat to shipping in the strait immediately raises fears of oil shortages, pushing up petrol and diesel prices around the world, including in South Africa.

That is why consumers, transport operators and businesses have been watching developments in the Middle East closely as concerns about higher fuel prices continue to grow.

Speaking on 702, foreign policy expert Dr Oscar van Heerden said it was still too early to celebrate any breakthrough.

“I think it’s too early to start celebrating,” he said, warning that there were still “very contradicting stories” about what exactly had been agreed to.

Trump hints at a deal that could end the war in Iran

Van Heerden said the immediate priority for both sides appeared to be reopening the Strait of Hormuz to ensure the free flow of oil.

“The USA is desperate for the Strait of Hormuz to reopen,” he said.

He explained that Iran appeared willing to gradually reopen the route over 30 to 60 days to help stabilise energy markets and bring prices down.

That matters to consumers worldwide because oil prices directly affect fuel costs, inflation, and the cost of transporting goods.

In South Africa, higher oil prices usually feed into petrol price hikes, increasing pressure on households already struggling with high food prices, electricity costs and interest rates.

Oil price shocks can also affect taxi fares, delivery costs and airline ticket prices.

Sanctions and nuclear tensions remain major obstacles

Despite signs of progress, major sticking points remain.

Van Heerden said discussions about sanctions relief and Iran’s nuclear programme were still unresolved.

Iran wants sanctions eased and access restored to frozen assets as part of any initial agreement.

The future control of the Strait of Hormuz is also still under debate.

Van Heerden said Iran believed the issue should ultimately be discussed between Iran and Oman, the two countries that control access to the waterway.

The Trump administration, meanwhile, remains focused on preventing Iran from developing nuclear weapons.

Van Heerden said Iran was trying to delay difficult negotiations around uranium enrichment until after a longer-term ceasefire agreement was in place.

Trump accused of using negotiation tactics

The foreign policy expert also suggested that US President Donald Trump was using hardline public statements as part of a broader negotiating strategy.

He said Trump’s comments about not rushing into a deal were intended to increase pressure on Iran, even though both sides appeared eager for an agreement.

“These are all part of the propaganda,” Van Heerden said.

Still, markets reacted positively to reports of progress.

The interview noted that oil prices fell to two-week lows while stocks lifted amid optimism surrounding a possible agreement.

For motorists and consumers worried about another sharp fuel price increase, that could prove to be the most important development of all.
