Peace deal may increase exposure to overcapacity, price pressure as Middle East, Asia chemicals production normalises

2026-06-22 18:20

inInternational Shipping News22/06/2026

The peace deal to end the war in the Middle East is likely to increase global chemical markets to underlying overcapacity and push chemical prices down as chemical production gradually returns to normal in the Middle East and Asia.

The ceasefire agreement between the US and Iran will initiate a 60-day negotiation period covering Iran’s nuclear program and potential sanctions relief. The Strait of Hormuz is expected to reopen gradually after mine-clearance operations are completed. Pakistan Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif said on social media platform X on Monday. Pakistan has served as a mediator between the US and Iran. The Strait of Hormuz will be “opening” on 19 June upon the signing of the deal with Iran, US President Donald Trump said in a post on Truth Social after Sharif’s announcement.

CHEMICALS OVERCAPACITY IMPACTAlthough the Middle East war cut off most chemical supplies from the region, and badly impacted production in Asia outside China, chemical prices have been falling sharply in recent weeks as China stepped in by increasing exports sharply.

For the week ending 12 June prices of key upstream commodities continued their downward direction. Many other chemical prices have also been falling across all key regions.

China has been ramping up its chemical exports to fill in gaps left by falling production caused by the Middle East war, with a huge rise in volumes sent to the rest of Asia since the conflict began.

March and April chemical exports from China to the rest of Asia grew by more than 70%, according to figures published in the ICIS Supply & Demand Database, with exports to the Americas and Africa also rising significantly.

In 2026 China is forecast to add 77 million tonnes of new chemical capacity, and with its own economy underperforming, producers have been seizing the chance to gain market share in other regions.

If the peace deal holds and chemical production in the Middle East and Asia gradually normalises, the global supply glut is likely to worsen as this material enters already oversupplied markets.

Global overcapacity is likely to reach 186 million tonnes this year, putting operating rates and margins under further pressure.

Temporary relief from low-cost Asia and Middle East imports for producers in Europe is likely to come to an end as trade flows normalise.

PRICE IMPACTFollowing sharp increases through March in many chemical prices caused by panic buying after the war began, values went into reverse as China boosted exports and downstream customers refused to pay the inflated prices amid poor underlying demand.With the prospect of more supply entering the market after the peace deal, customers may delay purchases and empty inventories in the hope that prices will fall further.

ICIS senior analyst Lorenzo Meazza pointed out that global polyethylene (PE) markets remain structurally oversupplied, with capacity exceeding demand by nearly 26 million tonnes in 2026. He said: “In spite of the peace deal, the war impact on energy costs had already triggered demand revisions, with consequences also on long term projections, impacting PE demand growth estimations in 2026 and 2027. This is likely to further exacerbate global oversupply conditions and capacity rationalization in 2027.”

Meazza added: “I suspect we will see more price erosion. If buyers were already taking a wait and see position, now this could become even worse because they are more certain that prices will fall.”

CRUDE OIL RAMP UP WILL TAKE TIMEICIS head of oil markets, Ajay Parmar, believes the peace is likely to hold – albeit with some potential blips along the way – but that it will take several months before Middle East crude oil supplies ramp back up.

Asked why he believes the peace agreement will hold, he said: “The US blockade has been really effective and that means that Iran’s crude oil storage tanks are nearly full, meaning they will have to reduce production soon.

“On the US side, treasury yields are particularly high – that makes borrowing much more expensive, and means that President Trump can’t get the lower interest rates he wants. Essentially, it is now very much in both sides’ interests to sign the deal and stick to it,” he added.

Once the Strait is de-mined Parmar estimates it will take 2-4 months for flows to crude oil logistics to normalize because of displaced ships and bottlenecks caused by a limited number of berths for loading and unloading.

For petrochemical feedstocks such as naphtha the delay will be longer. “First you have to raise crude oil production, sort out logistical issues and then raise refinery run rates,” Parmar said. “Only then can you send out that feedstock to petrochemicals producers to raise their run rates again in Asia. We think it’s going to be early next year before we see some form of normality once again across crude, oil products, logistic and petrochemicals markets,” he added.

CRUDE OIL PRICES MAY RISE FIRSTParmar believes the delay in getting Middle East crude oil and refining rates back up means oil prices may rise in the near term.“The reality on the ground is that supply is going to take a long time to actually hit the demand centres once again. We think this will actually help to see prices inch up once again in July compared to June,” he said.

“Then over time, Parmar expects prices to fall back steadily through the second half of the year. However ICIS forecasts they will stay in the mid to low 70s throughout all of 2027.” “Even if we get a normalization of the markets sometime early next year, we still don’t think oil prices are going to go back down to the levels that we saw pre-crisis in the $60’s/barrel range. “This is because we’ve had such a big decline in inventories – over one billion barrels of oil has been lost as a result of this crisis. There is simply far less buffer in the forward oil market than there was just a few months ago,” he added.

From 2028 the pre-existing oversupply is expected to dominate, bringing prices back down into the $60s/barrel range once again.

CHEMICALS WILL HAVE TO WAITAnother commentator cautioned that the resumption of chemical supplies from damaged plants could take a long time.

LyondellBasell CEO, Peter Vanacker, warned in an ICIS interview recently that chemicals are likely to be at the back of the queue for restarting, after energy and food.

“If the Strait would open again, what would happen? What would come first is definitely energy – crude oil, oil products, LNG. The second is food – fertilizers. For the third, which is chemicals, it’s going to take a while,” said Vanacker.
