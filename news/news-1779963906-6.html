EU Fertiliser Action Plan signals long-term reform; offers little immediate relief after Hormuz disruption

2026-05-28 18:25

inInternational Shipping News28/05/2026

The European Commission on 19 May announced its Fertiliser Action Plan, combining largely pre-announced measures to support farmer affordability with longer-term signals on market transparency, strategic autonomy, and the decarbonisation of EU fertilizer production.

The plan was published with EU nitrogen fertilizer prices that the Commission estimated to be around 70% above the 2024 average and 40% above December 2025 levels, after the Middle East escalation since 28 February has disrupted ammonia, urea, and sulphur supply from a region that accounts for a significant portion of global exports.

The Commission acknowledged that sustained high fertilizer prices risk curbing fertilizer use, with potential knock-on effects on crop yields and agricultural output. However, most of the plan’s near-term, market-affecting measures were already in motion before Tuesday’s announcement. The structural ambitions, including possible mandatory market data collection and a stockpiling assessment, depend on legislative steps that will play out over 2026-2028.

SHORT-TERM MEASURES LARGELY IN MOTIONThe Commission’s plan consolidates several measures already announced or proposed earlier in 2026. The Commission’s 20 February proposal to suspend Common Customs Tariff duties on ammonia, urea, and certain other nitrogen-based and mixed fertilizers from non-Russia/Belarus origins for one year – estimated to save EU importers around €60 million – remains pending Council adoption. The Commission has flagged the possibility of expanding the scope of products covered.

A temporary State Aid framework adopted on 29 April allows Member States to support farmers directly affected by the crisis at the Strait of Hormuz.

The plan also signals a targeted Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) package to enable liquidity support and more flexible advance payments to farmers under existing strategic plans, with the Commission proposing to mobilise the EU budget to substantially increase the agricultural reserve. The CAP package largely repurposes existing funding rather than introducing fresh subsidies.

On the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), a topic of debate, the Commission confirmed the existing flat 1% markup applied to default values for fertilizers. That is significantly lower than the 10%, 20%, and 30% markups applied to other CBAM goods in 2026, 2027, and 2028 respectively. A review of default values is committed to by December 2027 at the latest.

INDUSTRY, FARMERS REMAIN FOCUSED ON CBAMThe response from industry exposed the structural tensions underlying the European fertilizer sector. Fertilizers Europe director general Antoine Hoxha called the plan “a starting point, not a solution in itself”.

In a statement, Fertilizers Europe said the Commission’s plan “regrettably fails to address mounting structural pressures, including high and volatile energy costs, carbon leakage risks, and the regulatory burdens”.

The group is pushing for ETS flexibility in the upcoming review, including “a slower phase-out of free emissions allowances”.

Hoxha stressed that, “Under current technologies, organic fertilisers can replace up to 3% of currently applied N fertilisers making home-grown mineral fertilisers essential for Europe’s food security.” Copa-Cogeca, the EU farm lobby, called for a suspension of CBAM for fertilizers, zero import duties (excluding Russia and Belarus), and allowing Member States to derogate from Nitrates Directive thresholds for the next cropping season.

The group has estimated that CBAM could cost EU farmers up to €39 billion over seven years, framing that “today’s fertiliser crisis for farmers is tomorrow’s food crisis for consumers”. The Commission’s position on CBAM – keeping the mechanism in place with the existing 1% markup for fertilizers, and committing to evaluate how CBAM and ETS-related costs pass through to farmers and food prices – did not appear to satisfy either side.

TRANSPARENCY PUSHThe Commission’s plan sets out its intention to strengthen the Fertilisers Market Observatory and, if voluntary data collection remains insufficient, propose mandatory systematic collection of market information such as production volumes, capacity, sales prices, and stocks by Member State, through an amendment to the Fertilising Products Regulation “or any other appropriate Act”. This parallels the US Fertilizer Transparency Act of 2026, introduced in the US Senate on 19 March and the House on 26 March, which would mandate weekly USDA reporting of prices and quantities from manufacturers and wholesalers. Both proposals were triggered by the Middle East disruption; neither is yet law.

WHAT TO WATCH FOR GOING FORWARDThe Commission is committed to a legislative proposal to revise the EU emissions trading system by July 2026, with explicit language that “any additional flexibility for industry is conditional on increased decarbonised homegrown production”. A review of CBAM default values is due by December 2027.

For phosphate fertilizers, the Commission’s evaluation will include a review of the cadmium limit values, considering “the feasibility of reducing these limit values to a lower appropriate level”.

The Commission also confirmed work to extend the RENURE (Recovered Nitrogen from manURE) framework to digestates, with the aim of providing solutions “by the next growing season”. It remains to be seen how quickly Brussels moves from signal to legislation. For now, the Fertiliser Action Plan reads more as a forward map of where EU fertilizer policy is heading than a set of measures likely to materially move European prices in the 2027 sowing season.That, in itself, might be enough to justify the discontent some industry bodies were expressing.Source: Focus article by Chris Vlachopoulos, ICIS
