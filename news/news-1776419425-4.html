Why Iran is unlikely to give up its enriched uranium as Trump claims

Thu, 16 Apr 2026 23:40

US-Iran nuclear negotiations remain deadlocked over enriched uranium, with Donald Trump pushing for long-term restrictions while Tehran refuses to abandon enrichment rights. As ceasefire talks progress, the dispute over stockpiles, sanctions, and security guarantees continues to shape a possible breakthrough deal

Iran’s enriched uranium stockpile is a sticking point in negotiations between Washington and Tehran, even as both sides publicly signal interest in striking a deal to end ongoing hostilities.

Statements from US President Donald Trump suggest progress may be within reach, but positions on the most critical issue — uranium enrichment — remain sharply divided.

The United States is seeking verifiable guarantees that Iran cannot develop nuclear weapons, while Iran continues to defend its nuclear programme as a sovereign right tied to energy security.

Trump claims Iran ready to give up ’nuclear dust’

Speaking to reporters at the White House on Thursday, US President Donald Trumpclaimed that Iran had agreed to relinquish what he referred to as “nuclear dust,”a phrase he used to describe the country’s enriched uranium stockpile, including material believed to be buried at sites targeted in previous US airstrikes.

“They’ve agreed to give us back the nuclear dust,” Trump said, adding, “There’s a very good chance we’re going to make a deal.”

The US president characterised ongoing talks as a “very successful negotiation” and suggested that a deal would have broader implications beyond the nuclear issue.

According to Trump, such an agreement could lead to “free oil,” an open Strait of Hormuz, and an overall stabilisation of the region. He also indicated that he might travel to Islamabad if an agreement is finalised in the Pakistani capital.

In other remarks outside the White House and later in Las Vegas, Trump reiterated his belief that the conflict with Iran could conclude soon. “We’re going to see what happens. But I think we’re very close to making a deal with Iran,” he said, later adding that the war “should be ending pretty soon.”

These comments come against the backdrop of ongoing diplomatic efforts, including marathon negotiations in Islamabad that lasted 21 hours but ultimately failed to produce a final agreement last weekend.

Despite Trump’s optimism,sources on the Iranian side have cautioned that the talks are far from concluded,with key disagreements still unresolved.

For Tehran, uranium enrichment is not merely a technical component of its nuclear programme but a core national interest. Iranian officials have repeatedly pointed out that they will not accept any agreement that requires them to fully abandon this capability.

A senior Iranian official, speaking anonymously toUSA Today, stated clearly that there is no scenario under which Iran would agree to completely halt uranium enrichment as part of a deal with the United States.

This position is consistent with Iran’s long-standing approach to negotiations with Western powers, where its nuclear programme has been treated as a red line. Iranian authorities have maintained that their nuclear activities are intended solely for peaceful purposes, particularly electricity generation and energy security.

Tehran has outlined ambitious plans to expand its nuclear energy capacity to 20 gigawatts by 2041. However, the current reality of Iran’s nuclear infrastructure presents a stark contrast.

The Bushehr nuclear power plant, built with Russian assistance and operational since 2013, has a capacity of around 1,000 megawatts and remains the country’s only functioning nuclear facility. This accounts for roughly 1 per cent of Iran’s total electricity production.

To bridge a significant energy deficit — estimated at approximately 25,000 megawatts — Iran would need to construct around 25 additional facilities comparable to Bushehr.

Given that the existing plant took roughly two decades to complete, achieving such expansion would be a long-term and resource-intensive undertaking.

Despite these constraints, Iran’s accumulation of enriched uranium has raised concerns internationally. Prior to the US and Israeli airstrikes in June 2025, Tehran was believed to possess more than 400 kilogrammes of uranium enriched to 60 per cent and nearly 200 kilograms enriched to 20 per cent.

These levels are significantly higher than those required for civilian energy use and can be further refined to reach weapons-grade purity of around 90 per cent.

Iran has consistently denied any intention to develop nuclear weapons.

How enriched uranium is a red line for the US

For the United States, Iran’s enriched uranium represents a direct proliferation risk. Washington’s position is that even if Iran does not currently possess nuclear weapons, its existing capabilities could allow it to develop one within a relatively short timeframe.

This concern has shaped the Trump administration’s negotiating stance, which includes firm demands for restrictions on enrichment activities and the removal or neutralisation of existing stockpiles.

White House spokeswoman Olivia Wales articulated the administration’s position, stating that it “will never allow Iran to have a nuclear weapon.”

She also highlighted the use of economic and military pressure to bring Iran to the negotiating table, including a naval blockade aimed at securing freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz.

“The Iranians’ desperation for a deal is only increasing with President Trump’s highly effective naval blockade in effect," said Wales.

“The President is rightfully securing the Strait of Hormuz to ensure freedom of navigation for all ships travelling to non-Iranian ports, ending the Iranian extortion of the world.”

US Vice President JD Vance, who has played a leading role in the negotiations, reinforced the need for concrete safeguards rather than verbal assurances. Following the initial round of talks in Islamabad, Vance stated, “the ball is in the Iranian court.”

“It’s one thing for the Iranians to say that they’re not going to have a nuclear weapon. It’s another thing for us to put in place the mechanism to ensure that’s not going to happen,” he said.

Washington has proposed a 20-year freeze on Iran’s uranium enrichment activities, a demand that Tehran has resisted. Earlier proposals from the United States had gone even further, calling for a permanent end to domestic enrichment — a position Iran categorically rejected.

How possible suspensions have come into play

While the United States has pushed for a long-term suspension of enrichment,Iran has offered significantly shorter timelines.

According to reports, Tehran initially proposed a suspension lasting up to five years, with some discussions suggesting a range of three to five years. In contrast, Washington has insisted on a freeze lasting two decades, reflecting its desire for long-term assurances.

Trump also indicated that Iran had offered not to possess nuclear weapons for more than 20 years, though this falls short of the US demand for enforceable limits on enrichment and stockpile size.

The gap between these positions proved insurmountable during the recent round of talks in Islamabad, which ended without a deal.

Iran has also tied any potential concessions to the lifting of international sanctions, which have had a significant impact on its economy.

Details of these conditions have not been publicly disclosed, but Iranian sources have emphasised that progress in negotiations depends on Washington meeting these requirements.

Beyond enrichment limits, the question of what to do with Iran’s existing stockpile of enriched uranium remains unresolved. The United States has pushed for the removal of this material from Iranian territory, arguing that its presence poses an ongoing risk.

There have been indications that Iran might be willing to consider partial measures, such as exporting some of its highly enriched uranium. However, such steps appear to be conditional and subject to broader agreement on other aspects of the deal.

Complicating matters further is the physical location of much of Iran’s nuclear material. Following US airstrikes in 2025, a significant portion of the stockpile is believed to be buried beneath the remains of a mountain facility that was heavily targeted during the operation.

According to International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) Director General Rafael Grossi, Iran’s nuclear material is primarily stored at two of the three sites struck by US forces, an underground tunnel at the Isfahan nuclear complex and a cache at Natanz.

While US officials have claimed that the strikes destroyed or disabled many of Iran’s centrifuges, the country is widely believed to retain additional equipment and the ability to establish new enrichment facilities, potentially underground.

In an effort to break the impasse, Russiaproposed a plan under which it would take custody of Iran’s enriched uraniumand convert it into fuel for civilian reactors. The proposal was initially put forward in 2025 and revived during recent diplomatic efforts.

Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov stated that Russia was prepared to accept the material on its territory, describing it as a constructive solution to the crisis.

“This would be a good decision. But unfortunately the American side rejected this proposal,” he said.

The United States’ reported rejection of the plan highlights its preference for more direct control over the outcome, as well as its scepticism about arrangements that might allow Iran to retain indirect access to nuclear material.

Iran, for its part, indicated that any decision on such proposals would depend on the broader context of negotiations with Washington.

How Trump’s withdrawal from JCPOA led to current issue

The current standoff cannot be understood without reference to the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), a landmark agreement that sought to limit Iran’s nuclear programme in exchange for sanctions relief.

Under the JCPOA, Iran agreed to reduce its stockpile of low-enriched uranium by 98 per cent and cap enrichment levels at 3.67 per cent — far below weapons-grade.

The deal also extended Iran’s “breakout time” for producing sufficient fissile material for a nuclear weapon from a few months to at least a year.

The agreement also imposed restrictions on centrifuge use and allowed the IAEA to conduct extensive monitoring of Iranian facilities. However, the deal included expiration clauses that would lift certain restrictions after a set period, leading critics to argue that it only delayed rather than eliminated the risk.

In 2018, during his first term, Trump withdrew the United States from the JCPOA and reinstated sanctions targeting Iran’s energy, petrochemical, and financial sectors.

At the time of the US withdrawal, Iran was reportedly in full compliance with the agreement, according to both the IAEA and US intelligence agencies. However, following the reimposition of sanctions, Iran began scaling back its commitments.

From 2019 onwards, Tehran increased enrichment levels to 60 per cent, deployed more advanced centrifuges, and reduced cooperation with international inspectors.
