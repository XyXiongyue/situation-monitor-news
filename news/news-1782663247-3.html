DBCC revises inflation, oil, exchange rate assumptions

Sun, 28 Jun 2026 09:07

GOVERNMENT economic managers expect inflation to stay elevated until next year because of high global commodity prices and supply shocks arising from the Middle East conflict, the revised macroeconomic assumptions of the Development Budget Coordination Committee (DBCC) showed.

“Looking ahead, inflation rate assumptions are expected to follow a higher path due to elevated global commodity prices and supply shocks arising from the ongoing Middle East conflict,” read a memorandum issued by the Department of Budget and Management (DBM), the agency heading the DBCC.

The macroeconomic assumptions by the state’s economic team showed that inflation could average at 6 percent to 7 percent this year. For next year, economic managers expect inflation to average at 4 percent to 5 percent.

Inflation, based on these revised forecasts, is only seen to return to the 2-percent to 4-percent target band of the BSP in 2028.

Tied to the reset in the inflation forecast is the expectation of the economic team for the price of oil to stay elevated this year.

As the International Energy Agency (IEA) warned that global oil supply and inventories may take time to fully recover and stockpiles may remain under “significant strain,” the DBCC noted that global prices are still expected to be elevated based on “Dubai Crude Oil” (DCO) futures prices.

In view of these developments, the assumptions for the DCO prices were revised upward to US$80 to 100/barrel of crude oil (bbl) for 2026, US$70 to 90/bbl for 2027, and US$60 to US$80/bbl for 2028-2030,” the memorandum issued by the DBM read.

The DBCC also revised upwards its forecast on the peso-dollar exchange rates to P60 to P62 against the dollar over the medium-term. Government economists believes the local currency is expected to trade within this range until 2030.

“Looking ahead, the peso-dollar exchange rate assumptions were revised upward to P60.00 to 62.00/US$1 over the medium-term, as depreciation pressure could persist amid prolonged Middle East tensions, elevated domestic inflation risks, and subdued growth prospects that may weigh on market sentiment,” the DBCC noted.

Data from the Bankers Association of the Philippines (BAP) showed the Philippine peso closed at P61.29 against the greenback on June 26.

The rate remained unchanged compared to its previous finish on Thursday.
