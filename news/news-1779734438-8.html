Kenya Among 27 Countries Seeking Access to World Bank Crisis Funding Amid Middle East conflict

Twenty-seven countries have moved since the start of the Iran war to put in place crisis instruments that could quickly access funding from existingWorld Bankprogrammes, according to an internal document reviewed by Reuters.

The development comes as the Middle East conflict continues to disrupt global energy markets, strain supply chains, and hinder the delivery of essential goods such as fertilisers to developing nations.

Reuters reported that Kenya is among the countries seeking rapid financial support to cushion its economy from the fallout, particularly surgingfuel prices.

The internal WorldBankdocument indicates that while 27 countries have initiated the process of activating crisis instruments, the majority are still completing the necessary steps.

Three countries have already approved new instruments since the conflict began on February 28, but the document did not identify them.

According toReuters, the World Bank declined to comment on the document or the specific countries involved, while Kenyan and Iraqi officials confirmed their pursuit of rapid financial support from the World Bank.

What crisis instruments are available to countries?

The World Bank's crisis toolkit includes several mechanisms that allow member countries to draw on pre-arranged contingent financing, existing project balances, and fast-disbursing instruments.

According to World Bank President Ajay Banga, these tools could enable countries to access an estimated 20 billion to 25 billion. He further indicated that the bank could reorient parts of its portfolio to bring the total to 60 billion over six months, with longer-term changes potentially raising the total to around 100 billion.

Among the 101 countries that had access to some form of pre-arranged financing instrument, 54 had signed up to the Rapid Response Option, which allows countries to use up to 10% of their undisbursed financing.

The internal World Bank document viewed by Reuters showed that three countries had approved new crisis instruments since the conflict began, while the remaining 24 were still completing the process. The document did not name all the countries or specify the total amount of funds being sought.

What specific challenges is Kenya facing from the Middle East conflict?

Kenya, like many developing nations, has been hit hard by the surge in global energy prices triggered by the Middle East conflict.

The country relies heavily on imported fuel, and the price increase has had ripple effects across the economy, driving up transportation costs, inflation, and the cost of living for ordinary Kenyans.

Additionally, disruptions to global supply chains have affected the availability and cost of essential goods, including fertilisers, which are critical for Kenya's agricultural sector.
