Federal Reserve Weighs Rate Hike as Inflation Surpasses 2%

Wed, 20 May 2026 16:43

The Federal Reserve, the U.S. central bank, released Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) minutes indicating heightened discussions about potential interest rate hikes. As the Middle East crisis prolongs, driving up oil prices and inflation, a majority of Fed officials emphasized that prolonged inflationary pressures could necessitate raising rates.

The minutes from the FOMC meeting on the 20th stated, “A majority of participants stressed that if inflation remains persistently above 2%, a certain level of ‘monetary tightening (Policy Firming)’ may be required.” Monetary tightening refers to measures like raising benchmark rates to reduce the money supply. The Fed also noted, “An overwhelming majority of members viewed that the risk of taking longer than previously expected to bring inflation back to the 2% target has increased.”

Fed officials cited energy supply issues stemming from the Middle East crisis and tariff measures as key inflation risks. Consequently, many participants favored removing the ‘easing bias’ language—suggesting potential rate cuts—from policy statements.

Earlier, Neel Kashkari, president of the Minneapolis Federal Reserve Bank, along with Beth Hameck (Cleveland) and Lorie Logan (Dallas), publicly opposed including the easing bias clause during last month’s rate freeze decision. However, Stephen Miran, a director appointed by President Trump, expressed preference for a 0.25 percentage point rate cut.

With the FOMC minutes revealing growing internal support for rate hikes, the calculations for Kevin Warsh, the incoming Fed chair set to officially assume office on the 22nd, are expected to grow more complex. President Trump recently told media, “I will let Warsh do what he wants,” signaling he would not pressure for rate cuts.

According to the CME FedWatch Tool, the probability of the Fed raising rates by December stands at 53%. Speculation that rates will increase at least once before the end of this year has surpassed 50%.

In South Korea, the outlook for rate hikes has also intensified due to high oil prices driven by the Middle East crisis. Yoo Sang-dae, deputy governor of the Bank of Korea, recently stated, “It is time to halt rate cuts and consider hikes.” Kim Jin-il, a newly appointed Financial Services Commission member, added, “A modest rate hike as an ‘insurance’ measure would be appropriate.”
